## 1. Purpose

This policy defines how NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD) manages business exceptions that exceed normal operational authority and require executive escalation.

The purpose of this policy is to ensure that material service, supply, inventory, financial, operational, or cross-functional risks are escalated in a controlled, timely, and auditable way. This policy supports EDIP by defining when standard decision workflows are no longer sufficient and when elevated business review is required.

---

## 2. Policy objectives

The objectives of this policy are to:

- define when a case becomes an executive exception
- ensure high-risk cases are escalated on time
- protect service levels, revenue continuity, and operational stability
- prevent unmanaged local decisions in enterprise-level risk situations
- support consistent approval governance across teams
- provide clear escalation traceability for EDIP explanations and audit review
- improve enterprise decision quality under exceptional conditions

---

## 3. Scope

This policy applies to:

- replenishment and inventory-risk cases exceeding planner or manager authority
- supplier disruptions with significant business impact
- cross-region inventory allocation conflicts
- major service-level threats
- exceptional commercial actions with operational impact
- material risk events requiring multi-team coordination
- cases where local decision-making is not sufficient to manage exposure

This policy does not apply to:

- routine replenishment actions within standard authority
- normal receiving discrepancies handled at warehouse level
- standard pricing actions within approved commercial control
- technical incidents managed exclusively through IT incident procedures unless business exception impact requires executive review

---

## 4. Core escalation principles

### 4.1 Escalate risk, not inconvenience
Executive escalation is required for material business exposure, not for ordinary operational discomfort.

### 4.2 Timeliness matters
A correct escalation made too late can still create avoidable business loss.

### 4.3 Local review first, if safe
Cases should be resolved at the lowest appropriate authority level when safe and permitted. Executive escalation is required when exposure exceeds that level.

### 4.4 Evidence-based escalation
An escalated case must clearly state the business risk, evidence, alternatives considered, and reason local authority is insufficient.

### 4.5 Traceability is mandatory
All executive exceptions must have a documented escalation path, decision status, and responsible owner.

### 4.6 Cross-functional alignment
When a case affects multiple business functions, escalation must enable coordinated decision-making rather than isolated local action.

---

## 5. Definitions

### 5.1 Executive exception
A business case whose risk, impact, urgency, or governance sensitivity exceeds normal operational approval authority.

### 5.2 Escalation
The formal process of raising a case to a higher decision authority for review, direction, or approval.

### 5.3 Material business exposure
A level of service, financial, operational, customer, or governance risk significant enough to require elevated review.

### 5.4 Escalation owner
The person accountable for initiating and documenting the escalation.

### 5.5 Decision authority
The approved level of business ownership allowed to approve, reject, delay, or redirect an action.

### 5.6 Cross-functional exception
A case requiring coordinated decision-making across more than one business function, such as planning, procurement, warehouse, and commercial teams.

---

## 6. When a case becomes an executive exception

A case should be treated as an executive exception when one or more of the following conditions apply:

- service-level exposure is enterprise significant
- critical SKUs face meaningful stockout risk across key regions or channels
- supplier disruption cannot be managed within normal planning tolerance
- cross-region reallocation affects multiple business priorities
- financial exposure is unusually high
- operational constraints block standard recovery options
- executive or commercial conflict exists across teams
- approval authority boundaries are exceeded
- significant customer, brand, or contractual impact is possible
- major exception handling requires multi-team coordinated action

A case does not need to satisfy every condition above. One sufficiently severe condition may be enough.

---

## 7. Common executive exception categories

### 7.1 Critical inventory shortage
Use when major stockout exposure affects key SKUs, regions, channels, or customer commitments.

### 7.2 Supplier failure escalation
Use when supplier disruption materially threatens recovery timing, service continuity, or replenishment confidence.

### 7.3 Cross-region allocation conflict
Use when available supply is insufficient and allocation choices create competing strategic priorities.

### 7.4 Capacity or operations constraint escalation
Use when warehouse, transport, labor, or facility constraints prevent normal execution of recovery actions.

### 7.5 High-value financial exposure
Use when a decision creates unusual cost, margin, working-capital, or loss risk beyond routine limits.

### 7.6 Commercial-operational conflict
Use when pricing, promotion, or commercial direction conflicts with operational feasibility or inventory protection.

### 7.7 Governance-sensitive exception
Use when a case has unusual audit, approval, policy, or control sensitivity requiring senior review.

---

## 8. When escalation is not required

Executive escalation is not required when:

- the case can be safely resolved within standard authority
- supporting evidence is weak and local review should continue first
- the issue is routine and governed by normal SOP or policy
- the case is operationally inconvenient but not materially risky
- the matter is a technical defect requiring system correction rather than business exception approval
- the decision owner has full authority and sufficient evidence to act without escalation

Unnecessary escalation slows the organization and weakens governance discipline.

## 9. Standard escalation workflow

### 9.1 Step 1 — Confirm materiality
The escalation owner must first assess whether the case truly exceeds normal authority or tolerance.

### 9.2 Step 2 — Define the risk clearly
The case summary must explain:

- what happened
- what risk exists
- why local authority is insufficient
- what timeline pressure exists
- what business areas are affected

### 9.3 Step 3 — Gather supporting evidence
Relevant evidence may include:

- inventory-risk signal
- forecast and replenishment context
- supplier update
- receiving discrepancy pattern
- service-level exposure
- financial impact estimate
- warehouse or logistics constraint
- commercial instruction or campaign context

### 9.4 Step 4 — State options considered
The escalation should describe the practical alternatives already reviewed and why they are insufficient, risky, or blocked.

### 9.5 Step 5 — Submit the escalation
The case should be routed to the correct authority owner or executive review group according to business governance.

### 9.6 Step 6 — Record outcome
The final decision, owner, timing, and action path must be logged in the approved decision record.

---

## 10. Required escalation content

Every executive exception should include the following minimum content:

- escalation identifier
- business area
- case summary
- affected SKU, supplier, region, or channel where relevant
- material risk description
- urgency level
- evidence summary
- options considered
- recommended action
- escalation owner
- timestamp
- required decision authority
- final status when resolved

Poorly defined escalation requests reduce decision speed and clarity.

---

## 11. Urgency framework

### 11.1 Monitor-level exception
Important but not yet time-critical. Requires elevated visibility and scheduled review.

### 11.2 Priority exception
Material business risk exists and requires faster management review.

### 11.3 Urgent exception
Immediate review is required because delay may create major service, financial, or operational damage.

### 11.4 Critical exception
The case represents serious enterprise exposure requiring immediate executive coordination and controlled response.

Urgency classification should reflect the real timing risk, not emotional language.

---

## 12. Evidence standards

### 12.1 Minimum evidence expectation
Every escalation must include enough business evidence to justify why the case exceeds normal authority.

### 12.2 Stronger evidence for higher-impact cases
Larger exposure requires stronger supporting detail, broader review, or clearer quantified risk.

### 12.3 Evidence examples
Valid evidence may include:

- projected stockout timing
- supplier failure confirmation
- open PO unreliability
- warehouse capacity limitation
- cross-region demand conflict
- promotion-linked demand pressure
- financial exposure estimate
- repeated override or exception pattern

### 12.4 Weak escalation examples
Weak examples include:

- “this feels risky”
- “please review urgently”
- “team is uncomfortable”
- “not sure what to do”

These are not acceptable without specific business context.

---

## 13. Approval governance

### 13.1 Authority principle
Executive exceptions must be reviewed by the lowest higher authority capable of managing the exposure safely and lawfully.

### 13.2 Cross-functional review
If the case affects planning, procurement, warehouse, and commercial teams together, the escalation must support joint review rather than isolated approval.

### 13.3 Temporary approvals
Where immediate action is required, a temporary approval may be issued, but the final decision and rationale must still be documented.

### 13.4 Retrospective documentation
Emergency action does not remove the obligation to maintain full decision traceability.

---

## 14. Example executive exception scenarios

### 14.1 Critical SKU supply failure
A key supplier cannot deliver an essential SKU on time, existing stock coverage is low, and no local recovery path is sufficient. Escalation is required.

### 14.2 Cross-region allocation conflict
A limited supply pool must be allocated between regions with competing service priorities. Local teams cannot resolve the trade-off alone. Escalation is required.

### 14.3 Promotion versus inventory conflict
A commercial team wants to continue a high-visibility campaign, but inventory recovery is too weak to support demand safely. Escalation is required to resolve the trade-off.

### 14.4 Warehouse recovery constraint
Inventory is available inbound, but warehouse capacity or transport failure prevents timely availability. Escalation is required if service exposure becomes material.

### 14.5 High-cost emergency action
Recovery is possible only through unusually expensive or non-standard action. Escalation is required because financial authority is exceeded.

---

## 15. Roles and responsibilities

### 15.1 Planner or Local Decision Owner
- identify when the case exceeds normal authority
- gather initial evidence
- raise the case promptly
- avoid unmanaged local action outside authority

### 15.2 Supply Chain Manager
- validate escalation quality
- support risk interpretation
- coordinate with related functions
- approve within management authority when applicable

### 15.3 Procurement Leadership
- provide supplier context
- confirm supply recovery feasibility
- support escalation for supplier-driven exposure

### 15.4 Commercial Leadership
- explain campaign, pricing, or customer-impact considerations
- participate when commercial priorities conflict with operational feasibility

### 15.5 Operations Leadership
- assess warehouse, transport, and execution feasibility
- support coordinated operational response

### 15.6 Executive Decision Authority
- review material business exposure
- choose action path
- approve, modify, delay, or reject proposed action
- ensure the final decision is governed and documented

### 15.7 EDIP Decision Support Layer
- surface exception signals
- support evidence visibility
- record escalation-related traceability
- not replace executive accountability

---

## 16. Decision logging requirements

Every executive exception must be logged in the approved decision process.

### 16.1 Minimum logging fields
A valid executive exception record should include:

- escalation identifier
- case category
- affected business area
- affected SKU, supplier, region, or channel
- original issue summary
- risk level
- urgency level
- evidence summary
- recommended action
- decision authority
- final decision
- decision timestamp
- escalation owner
- follow-up status

### 16.2 Logging quality standard
The escalation log must be specific enough that an auditor, manager, or reviewer can understand both the risk and the final resolution.

### 16.3 Follow-up requirement
Resolved exceptions should have follow-up tracking where recurring risk, supplier action, or process correction is needed.

## 20. Final policy statement

NorthStar Retail & Distribution will manage executive exceptions through a timely, evidence-based, cross-functional, and auditable escalation process. When business exposure exceeds normal operational authority, the case must be escalated in a disciplined way so that enterprise decisions remain controlled, explainable, and aligned with service, financial, and governance priorities within EDIP.
