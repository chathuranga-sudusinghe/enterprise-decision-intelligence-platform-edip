# Promotion Execution Policy
## NorthStar Retail & Distribution
## File: docs/policies/promotion_execution_policy.md

## 1. Purpose

This policy defines how NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD) plans, approves, activates, controls, and closes promotional activity.

Its purpose is to ensure that promotions are executed in a commercially justified, operationally ready, and analytically traceable manner. This policy supports EDIP by defining the business controls required to connect promotions with price history, demand changes, inventory readiness, replenishment pressure, and post-campaign review.

---

## 2. Policy objectives

The objectives of this policy are to:

- ensure promotions are planned with clear business purpose
- align campaign execution with pricing, inventory, and supply conditions
- reduce avoidable service disruption during promotional periods
- prevent uncontrolled discounting or ungoverned campaign launch
- improve consistency in campaign activation and closure
- support explainable promotion-related reasoning within EDIP
- maintain strong auditability for promotion decisions and outcomes

---

## 3. Scope

This policy applies to:

- all enterprise promotional campaigns
- promotion-linked price reductions
- planned merchandising campaigns with demand impact
- regional and channel-based promotional activity
- promotion activation and deactivation controls
- business review of campaign readiness and closure

This policy does not apply to:

- supplier-funded commercial agreements outside internal execution control
- creative marketing content approval
- detailed media-buying procedures
- one-off unauthorized local discounting
- purely technical system-release procedures

---

## 4. Core execution principles

### 4.1 Promotion must have a business purpose
A promotion must be linked to a defined objective such as demand uplift, sell-through improvement, inventory reduction, customer acquisition, or campaign-based commercial support.

### 4.2 Readiness before launch
No promotion should go live unless pricing, inventory, and operational readiness are sufficient for controlled execution.

### 4.3 Promotion and price must stay aligned
A campaign must use the approved pricing structure and timing defined in the relevant pricing process.

### 4.4 Operational control matters
A commercially attractive promotion can still be a poor execution outcome if it creates unmanaged stock pressure, pricing confusion, or service failure.

### 4.5 Promotion timing must be controlled
Start dates, end dates, and any extension decisions must be governed and recorded.

### 4.6 Traceability is mandatory
Every material promotion must be documented clearly enough to support review, explanation, and audit.

---

## 5. Definitions

### 5.1 Promotion
A time-bound commercial action designed to influence customer demand through discounting, visibility, bundling, or other planned merchandising support.

### 5.2 Promotion window
The approved start and end period during which the campaign is active.

### 5.3 Promotion activation
The formal go-live of the campaign under approved execution conditions.

### 5.4 Promotion deactivation
The formal closure of the campaign, including end of active pricing and execution status.

### 5.5 Promotion readiness
The state in which campaign pricing, inventory, operational support, and governance approvals are sufficient for safe launch.

### 5.6 Campaign extension
A controlled continuation of a promotion beyond the original approved end date.

### 5.7 Unauthorized promotion
Any campaign, local activation, or material discounting activity launched outside approved policy, approval, or logging requirements.

---

## 6. Standard promotion execution workflow

### 6.1 Step 1 — Define campaign objective
Before approval, the campaign owner must define:

- target business objective
- affected SKU or category scope
- target region or channel
- campaign timing
- expected demand effect
- expected inventory effect
- required price structure

### 6.2 Step 2 — Confirm pricing alignment
Before launch, teams must confirm:

- base price is known
- promotion price is approved
- discount depth is controlled
- campaign price aligns with pricing policy
- effective dates are correct
- rollback to post-promotion price is planned

### 6.3 Step 3 — Confirm inventory and supply readiness
Before launch, teams must review:

- current stock position
- stock coverage against expected uplift
- inbound supply support
- supplier confidence
- warehouse receiving readiness
- regional availability where the campaign is targeted

### 6.4 Step 4 — Confirm operational readiness
Before launch, teams must confirm:

- campaign timing is approved
- execution scope is clear
- store or channel visibility is ready where applicable
- monitoring responsibility is assigned
- escalation route is known for service-risk cases

### 6.5 Step 5 — Activate the promotion
The campaign may be activated only after readiness, approval, and pricing controls are confirmed.

### 6.6 Step 6 — Monitor during campaign
During the active period, teams should monitor:

- demand uplift
- stock coverage
- replenishment pressure
- service-level exposure
- regional or channel variation
- pricing integrity
- unexpected operational issues

### 6.7 Step 7 — Close and review the campaign
At campaign end, teams must:

- deactivate promotion pricing correctly
- confirm closure timing
- review demand and inventory effect
- identify service or execution issues
- record any required follow-up action

---

## 7. Promotion approval requirements

### 7.1 Approval principle
Every material promotion must be approved through the appropriate commercial governance path before launch.

### 7.2 Approval content
The approval request should include:

- campaign objective
- scope
- timing
- pricing structure
- expected demand effect
- inventory readiness summary
- key business risks
- responsible owner

### 7.3 Higher-risk campaign review
A stronger review is required when:

- discount depth is high
- SKU criticality is high
- inventory availability is already tight
- campaign scope is enterprise-wide
- commercial visibility is high
- margin pressure is material

### 7.4 Unapproved activity prohibition
No team may activate a material promotion outside the approved process.

---

## 8. Pricing control requirements

### 8.1 Approved price structure only
Promotion pricing must match the approved campaign structure.

### 8.2 Effective-date control
Start and end timing for promotional pricing must be controlled and verifiable.

### 8.3 End-of-promotion rollback
The return to normal or next approved price state must be prepared before launch.

### 8.4 Overlap control
Where multiple promotional or markdown actions may overlap, the business owner must confirm how pricing will behave.

### 8.5 Price-history logging
Material promotion-linked pricing events must be recorded in the official price history process.

---

## 9. Inventory readiness requirements

### 9.1 Pre-launch stock review
Campaign launch requires review of whether inventory can support expected uplift without creating unmanaged service risk.

### 9.2 Low-stock caution
Promotions should not normally be launched on items already under material stock pressure unless a specific strategic reason is approved.

### 9.3 Supplier and inbound confidence
Open supply should not be treated as guaranteed support if supplier or receiving reliability is weak.

### 9.4 Regional readiness
Regional campaigns require review of local stock and local recovery capability, not only enterprise totals.

### 9.5 Post-launch monitoring
If inventory degrades faster than expected during the campaign, planners and commercial teams must review whether intervention is needed.

---

## 10. Service-level protection rules

### 10.1 Service-risk awareness
Promotions must be designed and executed with awareness of customer availability expectations.

### 10.2 Critical SKU caution
Extra caution is required when promoting critical or strategically important items.

### 10.3 Replenishment coordination
Promotions with expected demand uplift must be visible to planning and replenishment teams before activation.

### 10.4 Escalation trigger
If a campaign creates material service-level exposure, the case should move into priority review or escalation according to business severity.

---

## 11. Campaign timing and control rules

### 11.1 Controlled start
The campaign should start only on the approved date and scope.

### 11.2 Controlled end
The campaign should end on the approved date unless a formal extension is approved.

### 11.3 Extension governance
Campaign extensions require explicit review of:

- commercial rationale
- inventory condition
- service risk
- pricing effect
- operational feasibility

### 11.4 Delayed launch treatment
If the campaign launches late or partially, the event should be logged because timing distortion affects impact interpretation.

### 11.5 Early stop treatment
If the campaign must end early for operational or service reasons, the reason must be documented.

---

## 12. During-campaign monitoring rules

### 12.1 Demand monitoring
Teams should monitor whether demand uplift is:

- meaningful
- weaker than expected
- stronger than expected
- localized
- unstable
- difficult to interpret due to stock limitation

### 12.2 Inventory monitoring
Teams should monitor:

- coverage days
- rapid stock decline
- stockout proximity
- inbound recovery readiness
- region-specific stock pressure

### 12.3 Execution monitoring
Teams should monitor:

- pricing accuracy
- timing integrity
- campaign scope alignment
- store or channel execution gaps
- operational incidents affecting availability

### 12.4 Exception handling
Material campaign issues should be logged and routed to the appropriate planning, pricing, or escalation process.

---

## 13. Post-campaign closure rules

### 13.1 Formal closure
A promotion is not considered complete until the campaign is formally closed and pricing state is normalized or moved to the next approved state.

### 13.2 Post-campaign review
After closure, teams should review:

- observed demand uplift
- post-promotion fade or carryover
- inventory effect
- margin effect
- service pressure created
- execution quality
- repeatability of the campaign structure

### 13.3 Distorted-result caution
If stockouts, supplier issues, or timing problems affected the campaign, the result should not be interpreted too simply.

### 13.4 Follow-up actions
Where needed, the campaign owner should trigger:

- promotion-impact review
- replenishment review
- pricing review
- markdown review
- escalation review

---

## 14. Unauthorized or weak execution behavior

The following are not policy-compliant:

- launching a promotion without approval
- changing promotion price without governance
- failing to inform planning teams of major expected uplift
- extending a campaign without controlled review
- leaving promotional pricing active after campaign end
- launching stock-sensitive campaigns without readiness review
- failing to record campaign timing or scope clearly

These behaviors reduce commercial discipline and weaken EDIP explanation quality.

---

## 15. Common promotion execution scenarios

### 15.1 Controlled successful launch
Pricing, inventory, and timing were aligned. The campaign launched as planned and remained manageable. This is the preferred execution case.

### 15.2 Launch with low-stock pressure
The campaign launched correctly, but inventory tightened faster than expected. Planning review is required.

### 15.3 Delayed launch case
The campaign started later than approved. Timing distortion should be logged because impact interpretation may change.

### 15.4 Extension request case
The commercial team wants to continue the campaign. Extension requires review of stock, service risk, and business value.

### 15.5 Early stop case
The campaign is stopped before planned end because service risk or operational constraints became too high. The case must be documented and reviewed.

---

## 16. Evidence standards

### 16.1 Minimum evidence expectation
Every material campaign execution decision should be supported by evidence such as:

- approved campaign objective
- promotion window
- approved pricing structure
- stock readiness
- supplier and inbound support
- expected demand effect
- regional or channel scope
- operational readiness

### 16.2 Higher-risk campaign expectation
Broader or riskier campaigns require stronger evidence and clearer pre-launch review.

### 16.3 Unsupported launch rule
If the business cannot explain why the campaign should run and how it will be supported, the campaign should not be launched casually.

---

## 17. Logging requirements

Material promotion execution events should be logged in the approved process.

### 17.1 Minimum logging fields
A valid promotion execution record should include:

- promotion identifier
- campaign objective
- affected SKU or scope
- region or channel scope
- start date
- end date
- pricing structure reference
- owner
- execution status
- exception note where applicable
- timestamp
- escalation flag where applicable

### 17.2 Logging quality standard
The log must be specific enough that another reviewer can understand what ran, when it ran, how it was priced, and whether any execution issue occurred.

### 17.3 Weak logging examples
Weak examples include:
- “promo started”
- “discount live”
- “extended”
- “campaign ended”

These are not acceptable without business context.

### 17.4 Strong logging examples
Stronger examples include:
- “Promotion activated as approved for West and E-commerce channels with controlled discount depth and confirmed stock readiness.”
- “Campaign extension approved for three additional days after review of remaining inventory, continued demand uplift, and acceptable service risk.”
- “Campaign ended early due to rapid stock decline and rising critical-SKU service exposure.”

---

## 18. Roles and responsibilities

### 18.1 Category Manager
- define campaign business objective
- align scope with category strategy
- support campaign approval quality

### 18.2 Pricing Manager
- approve and control promotion-linked pricing structure
- ensure price-history alignment and closure readiness

### 18.3 Commercial Analyst
- support readiness review
- monitor campaign performance and execution quality
- document material observations

### 18.4 Inventory Planner
- review stock readiness
- assess replenishment risk
- support during-campaign intervention where needed

### 18.5 Supply Chain Manager
- review operational support and service-risk exposure
- support escalation when campaign execution creates broader risk

### 18.6 Store / Channel Operations
- execute campaign visibility and local operational requirements where applicable
- report execution issues

### 18.7 EDIP Decision Support Layer
- surface campaign, price, stock, and demand signals together
- support explainable execution reasoning
- maintain traceability support
- not replace governed human approval

## 21. Review and maintenance

This policy must be reviewed at least annually or earlier when major changes occur in:

- promotion operating model
- pricing governance
- planning coordination process
- service-level strategy
- execution-control requirements
- EDIP campaign decision logic

All updates must be version-controlled and approved by the responsible business owner.

---

## 22. Final policy statement

NorthStar Retail & Distribution will execute promotions through a controlled, commercially justified, inventory-aware, and traceable policy framework. Promotions within EDIP must be approved, launched, monitored, and closed in a disciplined way so that campaign outcomes remain explainable, operationally manageable, and aligned with enterprise pricing, planning, and service objectives.
