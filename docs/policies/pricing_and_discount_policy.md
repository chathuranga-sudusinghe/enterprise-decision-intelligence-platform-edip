## 1. Purpose

This policy defines the official pricing and discount governance rules for NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

The purpose of this policy is to ensure that pricing decisions are commercially sound, operationally controlled, analytically traceable, and aligned with margin, demand, inventory, and customer value objectives. This policy supports EDIP by providing the business rules needed to interpret price changes, markdown behavior, promotional actions, and commercial decision rationale.

---

## 2. Policy objectives

The objectives of this policy are to:

- maintain disciplined and explainable price governance
- protect margin while remaining commercially competitive
- use discounts in a controlled and purposeful way
- align pricing changes with inventory, demand, and commercial strategy
- reduce avoidable margin leakage
- support traceable decision-making for price history and promotion analysis
- provide grounded business context for EDIP recommendations and explanations

---

## 3. Scope

This policy applies to:

- all NorthStar retail pricing decisions
- all base price changes for managed SKUs
- all markdown and discount actions
- all promotion-linked price reductions
- all commercial teams, pricing teams, category teams, and approval roles involved in price decisions
- all price-related actions recorded in approved enterprise processes

This policy does not apply to:

- supplier-side contract pricing outside internal retail pricing control
- one-off manual billing corrections
- non-merchandise internal-use items
- prices controlled entirely by external regulatory mandate unless separately governed

---

## 4. Core pricing principles

### 4.1 Commercial purpose first
Every material price change or discount must have a clear business purpose.

### 4.2 Margin awareness
Pricing actions must consider gross margin impact, not only sales volume impact.

### 4.3 Controlled discounting
Discounts must be used as a strategic tool, not as uncontrolled revenue chasing.

### 4.4 Data-supported decisions
Price changes should consider demand patterns, inventory position, historical performance, and approved commercial context.

### 4.5 Traceability
All material pricing decisions must be explainable through business logic, approval path, and recorded price history.

### 4.6 Governance over convenience
Pricing decisions must follow approval and logging standards even when commercial urgency exists.

---

## 5. Definitions

### 5.1 Base price
The standard non-promotional selling price of a SKU.

### 5.2 Discount
A reduction from the active base price or standard sell price for a defined purpose.

### 5.3 Markdown
A price reduction typically used to accelerate sell-through, reduce excess inventory, or respond to product lifecycle risk.

### 5.4 Promotion-linked price change
A temporary price reduction tied to an approved promotional campaign or event.

### 5.5 Margin protection
The requirement to consider profitability risk before approving pricing actions.

### 5.6 Price history
The recorded timeline of pricing events, including start date, end date, previous price, new price, and decision reason where applicable.

### 5.7 Unauthorized pricing action
Any price-related change made outside approved process, approval authority, or logging requirements.

---

## 6. Pricing decision policy

### 6.1 Standard pricing decision logic
A price change may be considered when one or more of the following conditions apply:

- commercial competitiveness requires review
- demand performance is materially below target
- inventory overhang or slow movement requires action
- promotion strategy requires temporary price support
- lifecycle stage requires markdown planning
- margin opportunity exists through price optimization
- regional or channel conditions justify controlled adjustment

### 6.2 Required pricing decision inputs
All standard pricing decisions should consider:

- current base price
- recent sales trend
- margin profile
- inventory position
- forecasted demand
- promotion calendar
- product lifecycle context
- recent price history
- regional or channel relevance
- approval authority requirements

### 6.3 Prohibited pricing behavior
Pricing teams and systems must not:

- discount without clear business rationale
- repeatedly change price without review of prior impact
- apply deep discounts that create unjustified margin erosion
- bypass approval rules for convenience
- use inconsistent prices across channels without approved rationale
- create price history gaps by failing to log material changes

---

## 7. Base price governance

### 7.1 Base price stability principle
Base price should not be changed casually or excessively. Repeated price volatility weakens commercial discipline, customer trust, and analytical clarity.

### 7.2 Acceptable reasons for base price review
Base price review may be justified by:

- sustained change in demand behavior
- structural cost or margin pressure
- approved commercial strategy shift
- competitor pressure confirmed through review
- regional pricing adjustment need
- category-level pricing reset

### 7.3 Review expectation
Before changing base price, the owner should review:

- recent sell-through trend
- gross margin effect
- inventory exposure
- prior discount history
- expected customer response
- operational execution readiness

### 7.4 Recording requirement
All material base price changes must be recorded in approved price history records with effective date and responsible decision context.

---

## 8. Discount policy

### 8.1 Discount purpose
Discounts should be used only for defined business outcomes such as:

- promotion execution
- sell-through acceleration
- inventory reduction
- customer acquisition or retention support
- tactical commercial response
- controlled seasonal clearance

### 8.2 Discount discipline
Discounts must be proportionate to the commercial need. Larger discounts require stronger justification and appropriate approval.

### 8.3 Repeated discount risk
Repeated discounting on the same SKU without performance review is not policy-compliant because it may hide pricing weakness, reduce margin, and distort demand interpretation.

### 8.4 Discount end-date control
Temporary discounts must have a defined start and end period whenever possible. Open-ended discounting should be avoided unless explicitly approved.

---

## 9. Markdown policy

### 9.1 Markdown use cases
Markdowns may be used when:

- inventory is materially overstocked
- sell-through is persistently weak
- product season is ending
- discontinuation risk exists
- packaging or assortment refresh requires clearance

### 9.2 Markdown caution
Markdowns must not be used as a default solution without checking whether the issue is caused by:

- wrong forecast interpretation
- execution gap
- store allocation issue
- visibility issue
- temporary disruption rather than real demand weakness

### 9.3 Markdown review factors
Markdown decisions should consider:

- inventory aging
- expected recovery without markdown
- margin loss
- stock transfer alternatives
- regional demand differences
- category strategy impact

---

## 10. Promotion-linked pricing policy

### 10.1 Approved promotion alignment
Any promotion-linked discount must align with an approved promotion plan, commercial objective, and execution window.

### 10.2 Promotion readiness
Before activating promotion-linked pricing, teams should verify:

- SKU eligibility
- campaign dates
- inventory availability
- price communication readiness
- expected margin effect
- rollback timing after promotion end

### 10.3 Promotion end control
Temporary promotional prices must end according to approved schedule unless an extension is formally reviewed and approved.

### 10.4 Post-promotion review
Major promotion-linked pricing actions should be reviewed for:

- sales uplift
- margin effect
- inventory movement
- post-promotion demand normalization
- unusual demand distortion

---

## 11. Margin protection rules

### 11.1 Margin review requirement
Material price reductions must include a margin impact review before approval.

### 11.2 Deep discount protection
Deeper discounts require stronger evidence, because they can create permanent value erosion, difficult demand comparisons, and avoidable financial loss.

### 11.3 Exception handling
Where a lower-margin action is strategically necessary, the approval record must clearly state the business reason.

### 11.4 Margin-risk escalation
Pricing actions with unusually high margin risk should be escalated to the appropriate approval authority.

---

## 12. Inventory-aware pricing policy

### 12.1 Inventory signal usage
Pricing decisions should consider inventory position so that discounting and markdowns are used purposefully rather than blindly.

### 12.2 Excess inventory case
If inventory is materially above healthy levels and demand is weak, controlled pricing action may be appropriate after review.

### 12.3 Low inventory case
Discounts should not normally be applied to SKUs already under material stock pressure unless a specific strategic reason exists.

### 12.4 Replenishment coordination
Major pricing actions should be visible to planning teams because they may affect demand patterns, forecast interpretation, and replenishment decisions.

---

## 13. Price history governance

### 13.1 Recording rule
Every material price change must be captured in the official price history record.

### 13.2 Minimum fields
A valid price history record should include:

- SKU identifier
- region or channel scope where relevant
- previous price
- new price
- effective start date
- effective end date where applicable
- change type
- decision reason or pricing event type
- approval or owner reference where applicable

### 13.3 Analytical importance
Price history is required for:

- demand interpretation
- promotion impact analysis
- margin review
- pricing compliance monitoring
- model feature generation
- RAG-supported explanation

### 13.4 Missing-history risk
Unlogged price changes create major business risk because they reduce analytical trust, distort demand patterns, and weaken auditability.

---

## 14. Approval governance

### 14.1 Approval principle
All material pricing and discount actions must follow the approved authority structure.

### 14.2 Approval intensity
The required approval level should increase when:

- discount depth is larger
- margin risk is higher
- revenue impact is broader
- regional scope is wider
- promotion visibility is higher
- customer communication risk is greater

### 14.3 Emergency commercial actions
Urgent commercial situations do not remove the need for documentation. If a rapid pricing action is necessary, the action must still be logged and retrospectively validated according to policy.

---

## 15. Roles and responsibilities

### 15.1 Pricing Manager
- evaluate pricing opportunities and risks
- recommend base price and discount actions
- ensure pricing rationale is documented
- coordinate with category and commercial teams

### 15.2 Category Manager
- align pricing with assortment and category strategy
- review lifecycle and commercial context
- support decision quality for markdowns and promotions

### 15.3 Commercial Analyst
- analyze sales, margin, and price-impact patterns
- support evidence-based pricing decisions
- monitor pricing performance and anomalies

### 15.4 Inventory / Planning Team
- provide demand and inventory context
- flag where pricing actions may create replenishment or forecast implications

### 15.5 Commercial Leadership
- approve high-impact pricing actions within authority
- ensure pricing discipline remains aligned with enterprise strategy

### 15.6 EDIP Decision Support Layer
- surface price-history-aware insights
- support explainable pricing analysis
- highlight potential risk signals
- not replace human commercial approval where required

---

## 16. Compliance requirements

A pricing action is considered policy-compliant when:

- it has a valid business purpose
- required decision inputs were reviewed
- margin impact was considered
- price history was logged correctly
- approval rules were followed
- temporary discounts have controlled timing
- promotion-linked pricing aligns with approved campaign logic

Non-compliance includes:

- undocumented price changes
- repeated uncontrolled discounting
- deep discounting without rationale
- failing to end a temporary promotional price on time
- changing base price without proper review
- bypassing pricing authority rules

## 18. Review and maintenance

This policy must be reviewed at least annually or earlier when major changes occur in:

- pricing strategy
- margin targets
- category operating model
- promotion governance
- approval authority structure
- analytical or system design affecting price history controls

All policy updates must be version-controlled and approved by the responsible business owner.

---

## 19. Final policy statement

NorthStar Retail & Distribution will manage pricing and discounting through a controlled, commercially justified, margin-aware, inventory-aware, and traceable policy framework. All material pricing decisions must be explainable, logged, and governed within the enterprise decision process supported by EDIP.
