# Internal Operational Communication Guidelines
## NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD)
## EDIP / Phase 5 RAG Knowledge Layer
## File: docs/rag_source/internal_operational_communication_guidelines.md

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the internal communication standards for operational coordination across planning, replenishment, warehouse, store operations, procurement, and executive escalation workflows at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

The purpose of these guidelines is to ensure that internal operational communication is:

- timely
- clear
- action-oriented
- role-appropriate
- auditable when needed
- aligned with official planning and control policies

These guidelines support consistent communication across the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) and related business operations.

---

## 2. Scope

These guidelines apply to internal communication related to:

- forecast review activities
- replenishment decisions
- planner overrides
- inventory risk alerts
- supplier service issues
- warehouse receiving coordination
- pricing and promotion execution concerns
- exception handling
- approval workflows
- executive escalations
- cross-functional operational follow-up

This document applies to communication between teams such as:

- supply chain planning
- procurement
- warehouse operations
- store operations
- regional management
- finance support
- commercial teams
- data and analytics support
- executive stakeholders

---

## 3. Communication Principles

All internal operational communication must follow these principles.

### 3.1 Clear purpose
Every communication should clearly state why the message is being sent.

### 3.2 Action focus
Messages should identify required action, owner, and timing where applicable.

### 3.3 Business relevance
Operational communication should focus on business impact, not unnecessary detail.

### 3.4 Timeliness
High-risk issues should be communicated quickly enough to support action before service impact worsens.

### 3.5 Traceability
Important decisions, approvals, overrides, and escalations should be documented in appropriate systems or controlled records.

### 3.6 Role appropriateness
The level of detail must match the audience. Operational teams need execution detail. Executives need summarized impact and decision requirement.

### 3.7 Professional tone
All communication must remain professional, factual, and solution-oriented.

---

## 4. Standard Communication Objectives

Internal operational communication should usually achieve one or more of the following:

- notify a team of a business condition
- request a decision
- request operational action
- explain a recommendation or override
- escalate a risk
- confirm completion of an action
- document exception handling
- align teams around next steps

Communication that does not serve a business objective should be minimized.

---

## 5. Required Message Structure

For material operational communication, the following structure should be used whenever possible.

### 5.1 Situation
State what happened.

Example:
- forecast variance exceeded threshold
- supplier shipment delay confirmed
- replenishment recommendation was overridden
- promotion demand risk identified

### 5.2 Business impact
State why it matters.

Example:
- stockout risk for top-selling SKU
- receiving delay may affect weekend availability
- excess stock exposure may increase in West region

### 5.3 Required action
State what is needed.

Example:
- planner review required
- warehouse reprioritization required
- supplier follow-up needed today
- approval required before release

### 5.4 Owner
State who is responsible.

### 5.5 Timing
State the target deadline, review window, or urgency level.

---

## 6. Communication Types

NRD recognizes the following major operational communication types.

### 6.1 Informational update
Used when teams need awareness but no immediate action is required.

### 6.2 Action request
Used when another team or owner must take action.

### 6.3 Decision request
Used when approval, confirmation, or business judgment is required.

### 6.4 Exception notification
Used when actual conditions differ from expected operational standards.

### 6.5 Escalation message
Used when risk, delay, or business impact exceeds normal handling thresholds.

### 6.6 Completion confirmation
Used to confirm that requested action has been completed.

---

## 7. Communication Priority Levels

### Priority 1 — Critical
Used for issues with immediate or major business impact.

Examples:
- imminent stockout of critical item
- warehouse receiving failure affecting multiple stores
- executive exception requiring same-day review
- major supplier disruption
- severe promotion execution mismatch

Expected handling:
- immediate visibility to relevant operational owners
- escalation path activated as needed
- concise and direct communication

### Priority 2 — High
Used for serious but manageable issues requiring same-day or near-term action.

Examples:
- major forecast variance
- repeated planner override trend
- delayed replenishment for high-volume locations
- service-level risk emerging in one region

### Priority 3 — Standard
Used for normal operational coordination and routine follow-up.

Examples:
- daily planning notes
- standard exception review
- non-urgent clarification
- completion update

---

## 8. Audience Rules

### 8.1 Operational teams
Messages to operational teams should include:
- exact issue
- affected SKU/location/supplier if relevant
- specific next action
- time sensitivity

### 8.2 Managers
Messages to managers should include:
- issue summary
- decision need
- impact level
- owner status
- recommended next action

### 8.3 Executives
Messages to executives should include:
- concise summary
- business risk
- options or recommendation
- approval or direction needed
- timing urgency

Detailed low-level transactional data should be attached only when needed, not placed in the main executive message body.

---

## 9. Approved Operational Content Standards

Internal operational communication should include only the information needed for effective action.

Appropriate content includes:
- issue summary
- operational impact
- recommendation
- status update
- owner and due time
- relevant metric or threshold
- approved policy reference if needed

Avoid:
- excessive background detail
- blame-oriented language
- speculation without clear labeling
- unverified claims
- unrelated side discussions

---

## 10. Use of Metrics in Communication

When metrics are used, they should be:

- accurate
- current
- easy to interpret
- relevant to the decision
- presented with context

Examples:
- forecast error vs threshold
- projected days of cover
- service level risk
- supplier fill-rate issue
- delay duration
- inventory exposure estimate

Metrics should support action, not create confusion.

---

## 11. Decision and Override Communication

When communicating about recommendations, overrides, or approvals, the message should include:

- original recommendation
- final override or decision
- reason for change
- business context
- approval status where relevant
- next operational step

For significant overrides, communication should align with the planner override governance policy and relevant decision trace requirements.

---

## 12. Escalation Communication Rules

Escalation messages must include:

- escalation reason
- current business risk
- what has already been attempted
- what decision or support is now required
- who owns next follow-up
- required timing

Escalations should not be triggered for normal issues that remain within standard operational control.

---

## 13. Cross-Functional Coordination Guidance

When multiple teams are involved, the communication owner should ensure:

- one clear message owner is identified
- roles are not duplicated unnecessarily
- open actions are visible
- updates are shared when the status changes
- the final outcome is communicated clearly

Cross-functional threads should not become unstructured discussion chains without ownership.

---

## 14. Approved Communication Channels

Operational communication may occur through approved internal channels such as:

- controlled email workflows
- planning review meetings
- structured system alerts
- governed collaboration tools
- exception review forums
- executive briefing notes

Material decisions should not exist only in informal chat if they affect execution, approval, or audit traceability.

---

## 15. Documentation and Traceability

The following items should be documented in controlled systems or governed records when material:

- final replenishment decisions
- planner overrides
- high-impact exceptions
- supplier disruption actions
- executive approvals
- regional escalation outcomes
- major operational risk communications

Where required, communications should align with audit logging and decision trace policies.

---

## 16. Tone and Language Standards

All internal operational communication should be:

- professional
- concise
- factual
- respectful
- free from emotional or accusatory language

Preferred style:
- state facts clearly
- identify business impact
- propose action or decision need
- confirm owner and timing

Avoid:
- vague wording
- unnecessary urgency language
- blame statements
- unclear ownership
- unsupported assumptions

---

## 17. Examples of Good Operational Communication

### Example A — Action request
**Subject:** West Region replenishment review required for high-risk SKU group

**Message:**  
Forecast-adjusted demand for selected SKUs in West region now exceeds available cover threshold for the next review window. Replenishment recommendations have been generated, but two items require planner confirmation due to supplier lead-time concerns. Planning team to review by 2:00 PM today. Procurement to confirm supplier response status in parallel.

### Example B — Escalation note
**Subject:** Executive review required for regional shortage risk

**Message:**  
A supplier delay affecting three high-volume SKUs now creates projected stockout risk across priority stores in the South region before the next planned delivery cycle. Planning and procurement reviewed available options and recommend controlled allocation plus emergency transfer approval. Executive direction is required before 4:00 PM to avoid weekend service impact.

### Example C — Completion update
**Subject:** Warehouse reprioritization completed for inbound exception

**Message:**  
Receiving team completed priority reallocation for the delayed inbound shipment. Updated inventory positioning is now reflected in the operational plan. No further action required unless supplier ETA changes again.

---

## 18. Non-Compliant Communication Examples

The following are not acceptable as primary operational communication for material issues:

- “Please check this.”
- “There may be some issue with stock.”
- “Someone changed the numbers.”
- “Urgent urgent urgent.”
- “This is not my fault.”
- untracked verbal instructions for high-impact decisions
- informal chat-only approval for major override decisions

These examples are unclear, non-actionable, or not governance-ready.

## 21. Summary

NRD requires internal operational communication to be clear, actionable, timely, role-appropriate, and professionally documented.

Good operational communication helps teams act faster, reduce confusion, improve execution quality, and maintain alignment between recommendation logic, human decision-making, and business control requirements.
