# Store Execution Exception Communication Guide
## NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD)
## EDIP / Phase 5 RAG Knowledge Layer
## Version: 1.0
## Effective Date: 2025-01-01
## Review Cycle: Annual
## Owner: Retail Operations Excellence Office
## Approved By: Director, Store Operations

## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the standard communication approach for store execution exceptions at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that store-level execution issues are communicated in a way that is:

- clear
- fast
- action-oriented
- role-appropriate
- operationally useful
- traceable when needed

Store execution exceptions often affect shelf availability, promotional execution, pricing consistency, replenishment effectiveness, and customer experience. These guidelines help teams respond with speed and consistency.

---

## 2. Scope

This guide applies to communication related to store execution exceptions such as:

- shelf stockout despite recorded inventory
- delayed shelf replenishment
- missed promotion setup
- price label mismatch
- planogram execution issue
- receiving backlog affecting shelf fill
- store transfer not executed as expected
- incorrect store-level inventory adjustment
- damaged stock affecting availability
- urgent local execution disruption

This guide applies to communication between:

- store operations teams
- store managers
- regional operations
- replenishment planners
- warehouse coordination teams
- pricing teams
- merchandising teams
- procurement teams
- executive reviewers when escalated

---

## 3. Definition of Store Execution Exception

A store execution exception is any store-level condition where planned operational execution does not occur as expected and the gap creates actual or potential business impact.

Examples include:

- the system shows stock available but the shelf is empty
- a promotion was approved but not set up correctly in store
- price tags do not match approved promotional pricing
- transfer stock arrived but was not placed on shelf in time
- receiving activity delay prevents store availability
- damaged units reduce sellable stock unexpectedly

A store execution exception is not just a data issue. It is an execution gap with operational consequences.

---

## 4. Communication Principles

All store execution exception communication must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Speed with clarity
Urgent store issues should be communicated quickly, but the message must still be understandable and actionable.

### 4.2 Business impact focus
The communication should explain how the exception affects customer service, sales, promotional compliance, or inventory reliability.

### 4.3 Specificity
Messages should clearly identify the affected store, item, issue type, and immediate need.

### 4.4 Ownership
Each communication should show who owns the next action.

### 4.5 Escalation discipline
Exceptions should be escalated according to business impact, not emotion.

### 4.6 Traceability
Material exceptions and major decisions must be documented in the appropriate governed system or record.

---

## 5. Standard Message Structure

For material store execution exceptions, messages should include the following parts.

### 5.1 Issue summary
State what happened.

Examples:
- shelf empty although inventory is recorded in store
- promotion signage missing for approved campaign
- price label inconsistent with approved offer
- transfer stock not moved to selling area

### 5.2 Affected scope
State the affected business scope.

Examples:
- store ID / store name
- SKU or SKU group
- department or aisle
- campaign or promotion name
- date and time observed

### 5.3 Business impact
State why the issue matters.

Examples:
- lost sales risk
- customer dissatisfaction
- promotion execution failure
- inventory accuracy concern
- pricing compliance risk

### 5.4 Required action
State what is needed now.

Examples:
- store check required
- shelf refill required
- pricing correction required
- regional approval required
- planner review required

### 5.5 Owner and timing
State who will act and by when.

---

## 6. Common Exception Categories

NRD groups store execution exceptions into the following categories.

### 6.1 Shelf availability exception
The product should be available based on system records or delivery status, but the shelf is empty or underfilled.

### 6.2 Promotion execution exception
A promotion is approved, but the store has not executed it correctly.

Examples:
- display missing
- promotional shelf setup incomplete
- campaign item not visible
- signage not installed

### 6.3 Pricing execution exception
The in-store price display does not match the approved pricing or promotion.

### 6.4 Receiving-to-shelf execution exception
Stock has reached the store or receiving area, but it has not moved to customer-facing shelf space in time.

### 6.5 Inventory integrity exception
The store’s recorded inventory position appears inconsistent with physical reality.

### 6.6 Damage or quality-related exception
Sellable stock is reduced due to damage, expiry concern, or local quality issue.

### 6.7 Local disruption exception
A local store event or disruption affects normal execution.

Examples:
- staffing shortage
- local equipment issue
- temporary operational block
- urgent high-volume demand spike

---

## 7. Priority Levels

### Priority 1 — Critical
Use when the issue creates immediate business risk requiring rapid action.

Examples:
- high-selling item unavailable in major store
- major promotion not executed on launch day
- severe pricing mismatch affecting active campaign
- multiple-store issue with visible customer impact

Expected handling:
- immediate message to relevant store and regional owners
- same-day action requirement
- escalation if not resolved quickly

### Priority 2 — High
Use when the issue is significant but can still be controlled through same-day operational action.

Examples:
- delayed replenishment to shelf
- repeated stock discrepancy in one store
- transfer stock not executed within required window
- medium-scale promotion issue

### Priority 3 — Standard
Use for routine but relevant exceptions needing follow-up.

Examples:
- isolated shelf fill concern
- minor label issue
- non-urgent execution clarification
- completion follow-up

---

## 8. Audience-Specific Guidance

### 8.1 Store teams
Messages to store teams should include:
- exact issue
- exact location in store if known
- affected item(s)
- action required now
- deadline or urgency

### 8.2 Store managers
Messages to store managers should include:
- summary of issue
- expected business impact
- required local coordination
- owner accountability
- resolution deadline

### 8.3 Regional operations
Messages to regional teams should include:
- issue summary across store scope
- severity level
- whether pattern or repeated issue exists
- support or escalation needed

### 8.4 Planning or replenishment teams
Messages to planners should include:
- whether the problem is execution-only or demand/supply related
- current stock position if known
- transfer or replenishment relevance
- need for recommendation adjustment if applicable

### 8.5 Executives
Messages to executives should include:
- concise summary
- scale of impact
- current action taken
- unresolved decision need
- timing urgency

---

## 9. Required Content Standards

Good store exception communication should contain:

- store reference
- issue type
- affected item or campaign
- observed business condition
- business impact
- required action
- owner
- timing

Avoid messages that are:

- vague
- emotional
- blame-oriented
- incomplete
- unsupported by facts
- missing ownership

---

## 10. Store Execution Exception Workflow Communication

The normal communication flow should follow this order unless urgency requires direct escalation:

1. identify the issue
2. confirm basic facts
3. notify responsible local owner
4. communicate business impact
5. assign action owner
6. escalate if the issue exceeds local control
7. confirm completion or next review point

The message should evolve with the workflow, not remain static.

---

## 11. Escalation Rules

Escalation should occur when:

- the issue cannot be resolved at store level
- customer impact is significant
- promotion failure affects important commercial commitments
- pricing error creates compliance or brand risk
- the same issue repeats without correction
- multiple stores are affected
- the issue requires cross-functional action

Every escalation message should include:

- what happened
- what has been checked already
- what remains unresolved
- what support or decision is needed
- who owns next action
- by when the next update is due

---

## 12. Cross-Functional Coordination Guidance

Some store exceptions need more than one team.

Examples:
- shelf stockout with inventory mismatch may involve store operations, inventory control, and replenishment planning
- promotion execution issue may involve store operations, merchandising, and pricing
- receiving backlog may involve warehouse, transport, and store teams

In such cases:
- assign one communication owner
- keep the issue summary consistent
- avoid duplicated or conflicting requests
- share updates when the situation changes
- document the final resolution

---

## 13. Metrics and Evidence

When available, messages should include useful supporting evidence such as:

- current on-hand inventory
- recent delivery timestamp
- shelf status observation
- number of affected SKUs
- promotion start date
- price discrepancy detail
- number of affected stores
- photo confirmation or checklist completion reference if governed and approved

Evidence should support action, not delay action unnecessarily.

---

## 14. Tone and Language Rules

All communication must be:

- professional
- concise
- factual
- respectful
- solution-oriented

Preferred style:
- describe the issue clearly
- explain the impact
- ask for a clear action
- assign ownership
- set timing

Avoid:
- blame language
- unclear urgency
- unsupported assumptions
- overly long background detail
- vague phrases such as “please check” without scope

## 15. Example Messages

### Example A — Shelf availability issue
**Subject:** Immediate shelf check required for empty shelf with positive stock record

**Message:**  
Store 118 shows available inventory for SKU 44219, but the shelf was observed empty during the 10:30 AM floor check. This creates same-day lost sales risk for a high-velocity item. Store operations to confirm backroom status and refill shelf by 11:30 AM. Regional operations to be updated if stock cannot be located.

### Example B — Promotion execution issue
**Subject:** Promotion setup incomplete for weekend campaign launch

**Message:**  
Store 205 has not completed front-of-store setup for the approved weekend beverage promotion. Display materials are missing and two core SKUs are not positioned in the promotional area. This creates launch-day execution risk and potential sales loss. Store manager to complete setup by 2:00 PM and confirm with photo-based checklist submission under approved process.

### Example C — Pricing mismatch
**Subject:** Price correction required for active campaign item

**Message:**  
Store 087 currently shows shelf pricing for SKU 90871 that does not match the approved campaign price effective today. This creates pricing compliance risk and possible customer dissatisfaction. Pricing support and store operations to correct labels before 1:00 PM and confirm completion.

### Example D — Escalation
**Subject:** Regional escalation required for repeated store execution failure

**Message:**  
Three stores in the West region have repeated shelf availability exceptions for the same promoted SKU despite completed replenishment deliveries. Local checks suggest the issue is related to receiving-to-shelf execution delay rather than supply shortage. Regional operations review is required today to assign corrective action across affected stores.

---

## 16. Non-Compliant Message Examples

These are poor examples of store exception communication:

- “Stock problem in one shop.”
- “Promo not working.”
- “Please fix urgently.”
- “Something is wrong with shelf stock.”
- “This happened again.”
- “Why did your team do this?”

These examples fail because they do not identify scope, impact, ownership, or required action.

---

## 17. Link to Governance and Traceability

Material store execution exceptions should align with:

- internal operational communication guidelines
- audit logging and decision trace policy
- promotion execution policy
- planner override governance policy where planning decisions are affected
- executive escalation policy where issue severity requires higher approval or intervention

When the exception changes a formal planning or replenishment decision, the final decision must be logged in the proper controlled system.

NRD requires store execution exceptions to be communicated clearly, quickly, and with direct ownership.
