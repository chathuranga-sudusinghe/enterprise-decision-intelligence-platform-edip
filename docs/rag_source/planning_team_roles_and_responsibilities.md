## 1. Purpose

This document defines the official planning-team roles and responsibilities for NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to clarify who is responsible for forecast review, replenishment decisions, planner overrides, exception handling, inventory-risk monitoring, supplier coordination, and escalation support. This document supports EDIP by creating a clear governance structure so that decision flows remain explainable, auditable, and operationally controlled.

---

## 2. Document objectives

The objectives of this document are to:

- define the core planning roles used across NRD
- clarify accountability boundaries between planning and related teams
- reduce role confusion during normal and exception-based decisions
- support controlled handoffs across demand, inventory, procurement, warehouse, and commercial functions
- improve decision quality and operational discipline
- support grounded RAG explanations about who owns which planning action
- strengthen traceability and governance within EDIP

---

## 3. Scope

This document applies to:

- enterprise planning roles within NorthStar
- forecast review and demand interpretation activity
- replenishment review and inventory protection decisions
- planner override and exception handling workflows
- regional inventory-risk review
- coordination with procurement, warehouse, and commercial teams
- decision logging, review, and escalation support

This document does not apply to:

- detailed HR policy matters
- payroll or employee-grade structure
- technical software engineering responsibilities
- legal contract ownership outside planning governance
- store-level labor scheduling outside planning scope

---

## 4. Core planning governance principles

### 4.1 Clear ownership matters
Every material planning action must have a clearly understood responsible owner.

### 4.2 Decision accountability must be explicit
Teams may collaborate, but accountability for the final action must remain identifiable.

### 4.3 Planning is cross-functional
Planning quality depends on coordination across demand, inventory, procurement, warehouse, pricing, and operations.

### 4.4 Role clarity reduces weak decisions
When role boundaries are unclear, teams may duplicate work, skip review, or make unmanaged decisions.

### 4.5 Escalation is part of strong planning
Escalation is not failure. It is the correct control when risk exceeds local authority or information confidence.

### 4.6 Traceability is required
Material planning decisions must be attributable to the relevant role, timing, and business rationale.

## 5. Planning team structure overview

The core planning governance model includes the following roles:

- Demand Planner
- Inventory Planner
- Inventory Analyst
- Regional Planning Lead
- Supply Chain Planning Manager
- Procurement Partner
- Warehouse / Operations Partner
- Commercial / Pricing Partner
- Executive Review Sponsor where needed

These roles may vary by team size, but the functional responsibilities remain required.

---

## 6. Demand Planner role

### 6.1 Purpose of the role
The Demand Planner is responsible for reviewing forecast quality, interpreting demand patterns, and supporting business-aware forecast usage.

### 6.2 Core responsibilities
The Demand Planner is responsible for:

- reviewing forecast outputs
- identifying forecast exceptions
- assessing demand stability or volatility
- distinguishing structural change from temporary distortion
- considering promotion and pricing context in forecast interpretation
- supporting forecast-related review notes and evidence
- escalating high-impact forecast issues when required

### 6.3 Key decision responsibilities
The Demand Planner typically owns or leads:

- forecast acceptance, monitor, adjust, or escalate classification
- demand-pattern commentary
- forecast-risk interpretation
- demand-related planning input for replenishment review

### 6.4 What the role does not own alone
The Demand Planner does not independently own:

- final replenishment quantity decisions
- supplier recovery decisions
- warehouse capacity decisions
- executive exception approvals

---

## 7. Inventory Planner role

### 7.1 Purpose of the role
The Inventory Planner is responsible for reviewing inventory position and deciding the correct replenishment path under governed business rules.

### 7.2 Core responsibilities
The Inventory Planner is responsible for:

- reviewing replenishment recommendations
- assessing stock coverage and safety stock risk
- interpreting inbound recovery confidence
- considering supplier timing and warehouse readiness
- deciding whether to take no action, monitor, replenish, prioritize, or escalate
- applying controlled overrides where justified and authorized
- protecting service levels while avoiding unnecessary excess stock

### 7.3 Key decision responsibilities
The Inventory Planner typically owns or leads:

- replenishment review
- urgency classification
- quantity review within authority
- planner override execution within governance limits
- local inventory-risk response
- decision logging for material replenishment cases

### 7.4 What the role does not own alone
The Inventory Planner does not independently own:

- supplier contract resolution
- commercial pricing policy decisions
- warehouse policy design
- executive approval for enterprise-level exception cases

---

## 8. Inventory Analyst role

### 8.1 Purpose of the role
The Inventory Analyst supports planning quality through evidence preparation, risk visibility, and pattern analysis.

### 8.2 Core responsibilities
The Inventory Analyst is responsible for:

- analyzing stock-risk signals
- tracking recurring exceptions
- supporting evidence for planner decisions
- investigating repeated stockout or overstock patterns
- monitoring override trends
- supporting auditability and reporting
- identifying areas where model or rule improvement may be needed

### 8.3 Key support responsibilities
The Inventory Analyst typically supports:

- forecast and replenishment review evidence
- regional risk analysis
- supplier-linked inventory-risk interpretation
- decision-quality review
- governance reporting

### 8.4 What the role does not own alone
The Inventory Analyst usually does not independently approve final business action unless explicitly authorized.

---

## 9. Regional Planning Lead role

### 9.1 Purpose of the role
The Regional Planning Lead ensures that local market, service, and operational conditions are reflected in planning decisions.

### 9.2 Core responsibilities
The Regional Planning Lead is responsible for:

- reviewing regional demand and inventory conditions
- identifying local exception patterns
- supporting allocation and service-priority interpretation
- coordinating with regional operations and store-facing teams
- highlighting where enterprise averages do not reflect local reality
- recommending regional escalation when needed

### 9.3 Key decision responsibilities
The Regional Planning Lead typically owns or leads:

- regional risk commentary
- local service-risk interpretation
- region-specific planning coordination
- support for allocation review between regions

### 9.4 What the role does not own alone
The Regional Planning Lead does not independently own enterprise-wide trade-off approval unless formally authorized.

---

## 10. Supply Chain Planning Manager role

### 10.1 Purpose of the role
The Supply Chain Planning Manager provides governance leadership over planning quality, exception handling, and escalated decision review.

### 10.2 Core responsibilities
The Supply Chain Planning Manager is responsible for:

- reviewing high-impact planning cases
- approving or rejecting escalated local decisions within authority
- ensuring policy compliance in forecast, replenishment, and override activity
- coordinating planning alignment across functions
- identifying systemic process or control issues
- supporting management review of repeated exception patterns
- ensuring planning governance remains effective

### 10.3 Key decision responsibilities
The Supply Chain Planning Manager typically owns or leads:

- high-impact override review
- escalated replenishment review
- planning control oversight
- governance-quality review
- corrective action direction for repeated planning weakness

### 10.4 What the role does not own alone
The manager may still need executive approval when the case exceeds management authority.

---

## 11. Procurement Partner role

### 11.1 Purpose of the role
The Procurement Partner provides supplier-side context required for reliable planning decisions.

### 11.2 Core responsibilities
The Procurement Partner is responsible for:

- confirming supplier lead-time expectations
- identifying supplier disruption, shortage, or fulfillment risk
- supporting inbound reliability interpretation
- coordinating supplier-side issue clarification
- supporting corrective action for poor supplier performance
- contributing evidence for override or escalation decisions

### 11.3 Key support responsibilities
The Procurement Partner supports:

- supplier-risk assessment
- inbound confidence review
- partial shipment interpretation
- executive exception review where supplier exposure is material

### 11.4 What the role does not own alone
The Procurement Partner does not independently own final inventory-planning decisions unless separately assigned.

---

## 12. Warehouse / Operations Partner role

### 12.1 Purpose of the role
The Warehouse / Operations Partner provides execution reality for receiving, storage, intake capacity, and availability timing.

### 12.2 Core responsibilities
The Warehouse / Operations Partner is responsible for:

- reporting receiving delays or operational constraints
- validating practical inventory availability timing
- supporting discrepancy and damage interpretation
- identifying local intake or handling limitations
- providing execution feasibility context during planning review
- supporting service-risk and recovery interpretation

### 12.3 Key support responsibilities
The Warehouse / Operations Partner supports:

- replenishment feasibility review
- urgent recovery assessment
- inbound timing confidence review
- escalation cases affected by operational constraints

### 12.4 What the role does not own alone
The Warehouse / Operations Partner does not independently own commercial or planning-governance decisions outside their operational authority.

---

## 13. Commercial / Pricing Partner role

### 13.1 Purpose of the role
The Commercial / Pricing Partner ensures that planning decisions reflect promotion, price change, markdown, and category-strategy context.

### 13.2 Core responsibilities
The Commercial / Pricing Partner is responsible for:

- clarifying campaign timing and scope
- confirming price-change context
- supporting interpretation of promotion-driven demand changes
- identifying where commercial activity may distort forecast or replenishment signals
- contributing to post-promotion review and demand commentary
- supporting cross-functional resolution where commercial goals affect planning risk

### 13.3 Key support responsibilities
The Commercial / Pricing Partner supports:

- promotion-impact review
- price-history-aware planning interpretation
- commercial-execution review
- escalation cases involving commercial-operational conflict

### 13.4 What the role does not own alone
The Commercial / Pricing Partner does not independently override governed planning decisions without the appropriate planning and approval process.

---

## 14. Executive Review Sponsor role

### 14.1 Purpose of the role
The Executive Review Sponsor provides final decision support for enterprise-level exceptions that exceed normal operational authority.

### 14.2 Core responsibilities
The Executive Review Sponsor is responsible for:

- reviewing major cross-functional risk cases
- approving or redirecting enterprise-level exception actions
- resolving high-impact trade-offs
- ensuring major exceptions remain governed and documented

### 14.3 When this role becomes active
This role is typically involved when:

- service exposure is enterprise significant
- financial or brand risk is unusually high
- allocation conflicts affect multiple regions
- cross-functional disagreement prevents safe local resolution
- executive exception escalation is required

## 15. Responsibility mapping by decision area

### 15.1 Forecast review
Primary owner:
- Demand Planner

Key contributors:
- Inventory Planner
- Commercial / Pricing Partner
- Inventory Analyst

Escalation owner:
- Supply Chain Planning Manager

### 15.2 Replenishment decision review
Primary owner:
- Inventory Planner

Key contributors:
- Demand Planner
- Procurement Partner
- Warehouse / Operations Partner
- Inventory Analyst

Escalation owner:
- Supply Chain Planning Manager

### 15.3 Planner override handling
Primary owner:
- Inventory Planner

Key contributors:
- Inventory Analyst
- Procurement Partner
- Warehouse / Operations Partner
- Commercial / Pricing Partner where relevant

Approval / escalation owner:
- Supply Chain Planning Manager or higher authority

### 15.4 Regional inventory-risk review
Primary owner:
- Regional Planning Lead

Key contributors:
- Inventory Planner
- Inventory Analyst
- Procurement Partner
- Warehouse / Operations Partner

Escalation owner:
- Supply Chain Planning Manager

### 15.5 Supplier-linked planning issue
Primary owner:
- Procurement Partner for supplier context
- Inventory Planner for planning action

Escalation owner:
- Supply Chain Planning Manager or Procurement Leadership depending on issue type

### 15.6 Promotion-linked planning issue
Primary owner:
- Commercial / Pricing Partner for campaign context
- Demand Planner and Inventory Planner for planning response

Escalation owner:
- Supply Chain Planning Manager or Commercial Leadership depending on severity

## 16. Accountability rules

### 16.1 One named owner per material decision
Every material planning case must have one clearly identifiable responsible owner.

### 16.2 Contributors support, but do not replace ownership
Cross-functional input is important, but it must not create ownership confusion.

### 16.3 Approvals must be explicit
When a case requires approval, the approving role must be identifiable in the decision record.

### 16.4 Escalation must be attributable
The person initiating escalation must be clear in the case log.

### 16.5 Decision logging is part of ownership
A planning role is not considered complete if the decision was made but not properly logged.

---

## 17. Collaboration rules

### 17.1 Demand and inventory planning must stay connected
Forecast interpretation and replenishment action must not operate in isolation.

### 17.2 Procurement must be involved when supply confidence is weak
Supplier-related planning action should not rely on assumption alone.

### 17.3 Warehouse context must be included when execution timing matters
Shipment arrival alone is not enough when receiving constraints exist.

### 17.4 Commercial context must be included when price or promotion affects demand
Planning decisions must reflect campaign timing and pricing behavior where relevant.

### 17.5 Escalation should be collaborative, not delayed by debate
When a case exceeds local authority, the team should escalate with evidence rather than delay action.

---

## 18. Role-based compliance expectations

Each planning-related role is expected to:

- act within authority
- support evidence-based decisions
- maintain timely documentation
- participate in cross-functional review when relevant
- escalate when risk exceeds local control
- avoid unmanaged or undocumented material decisions

Non-compliance includes:

- acting outside authority
- failing to document material decisions
- ignoring required cross-functional input
- repeated unmanaged override behavior
- unclear ownership in high-risk cases

---

## 19. Common role-confusion scenarios

### 19.1 Forecast versus replenishment confusion
The Demand Planner interprets forecast quality, but the Inventory Planner owns the replenishment action path.

### 19.2 Supplier issue confusion
Procurement confirms supplier risk, but the Inventory Planner still owns the local planning response unless escalation changes the authority path.

### 19.3 Regional risk confusion
The Regional Planning Lead provides local context and coordination, but enterprise-level trade-offs may require management or executive review.

### 19.4 Promotion-impact confusion
Commercial teams explain campaign intent and pricing context, but planning teams own the inventory and replenishment implications.

### 19.5 Escalation confusion
A manager or executive may approve the final action, but the escalation owner must still document why the case was raised.
