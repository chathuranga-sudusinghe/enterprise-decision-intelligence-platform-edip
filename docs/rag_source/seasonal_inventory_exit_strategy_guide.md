## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the standard approach for planning, reviewing, approving, and executing seasonal inventory exit strategies at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that seasonal inventory is exited in a controlled, timely, and business-focused way before end-of-season stock becomes excess, creates avoidable markdown pressure, weakens working capital efficiency, reduces warehouse flexibility, or causes repeated seasonal planning inefficiency.

This guide supports the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) by providing governed knowledge for:

- seasonal inventory exit planning
- end-of-season stock recovery
- markdown and transfer support decisions
- inbound and replenishment wind-down control
- commercial recovery coordination
- escalation and approval support
- audit-aware decision traceability

## 2. Scope

This guide applies to seasonal inventory exit situations involving:

- end-of-season stock remaining above target
- narrowing seasonal demand windows
- residual campaign or event-related seasonal stock
- post-peak seasonal inventory recovery
- warehouse-held seasonal overhang
- store-level seasonal excess
- regional seasonal stock concentration
- inbound supply still planned as seasonality declines
- markdown-supported seasonal recovery
- repeated seasonal exit weakness across similar item groups or periods

This guide applies to teams such as:

- commercial planning

- warehouse operations
- regional operations
- store operations
- inventory control
- finance support where value exposure is relevant
- executive stakeholders for major seasonal recovery-risk cases

## 3. Definition of Seasonal Inventory Exit Strategy

A seasonal inventory exit strategy is a controlled business plan used to reduce or remove inventory that is tied to a limited seasonal demand window before or shortly after that demand window closes.

Examples include:

- reducing remaining summer product inventory before seasonal demand drops
- managing holiday residual stock after the main trading window ends
- adjusting replenishment and inbound for seasonal categories as demand declines
- deciding whether seasonal stock should be sold through, transferred, marked down, or recovered through alternate channels
- exiting weather-sensitive items before relevance falls sharply

A seasonal inventory exit strategy is not only a markdown decision.  
It is a coordinated commercial, inventory, and operational recovery process.

---

## 4. Core Principles

All seasonal inventory exit actions must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Start exit planning before the season ends
The business should plan exit early enough to avoid last-minute reactive behavior.

### 4.2 Focus on business impact
Exit actions must consider margin, working capital, warehouse pressure, inventory aging, recovery speed, and realistic demand decay.

### 4.3 Match exit intensity to remaining demand window
The shorter the remaining demand window, the more disciplined and decisive the exit plan should be.

### 4.4 Use the least costly effective recovery path first
Teams should consider normal sell-through, replenishment reduction, transfer, and targeted action before deeper commercial recovery steps.

### 4.5 Coordinate across functions
Material seasonal exit cases often require commercial, pricing, planning, warehouse, store, and finance-aware coordination.

### 4.6 Preserve traceability
Major exit decisions, markdown actions, transfer plans, inbound changes, and escalations must be documented appropriately.

## 5. Typical Reasons for Seasonal Exit Action

Seasonal inventory exit action may be triggered by:

- seasonal demand window narrowing faster than expected
- forecast overestimation of seasonal demand
- weaker-than-expected campaign or event response
- weather shift reducing relevance
- over-positioned seasonal stock before peak period
- inbound still in flow as demand slows
- regional imbalance in seasonal demand
- poor store sell-through
- warehouse concentration of seasonal inventory
- repeated slow recovery after similar seasons
- delayed markdown or transfer action
- product relevance decline after the key seasonal moment passes

The actual reason should be clearly validated before major exit action is approved.

---

## 6. Common Exit Strategy Categories

### 6.1 Controlled sell-through exit
Inventory can still recover largely through normal demand with controlled replenishment reduction and monitoring.

### 6.2 Transfer-supported exit
Seasonal stock can be rebalanced toward locations with healthier remaining demand.

### 6.3 Markdown-supported exit
Inventory recovery likely requires controlled markdown to accelerate sell-through.

### 6.4 Inbound wind-down exit
Future inbound or replenishment must be reduced, delayed, or stopped to avoid worsening exposure.

### 6.5 Warehouse release exit
Warehouse-held seasonal inventory requires structured release, redirection, or containment action.

### 6.6 Region-targeted exit
Seasonal recovery actions must differ by region because seasonal demand decay is not uniform.

### 6.7 Structural seasonal exit review
Repeated seasonal overhang indicates broader forecasting, positioning, or commercial design weakness.

---

## 7. Trigger Conditions

A seasonal stock case should be treated as a formal exit-strategy review when one or more of the following apply:

- projected cover exceeds end-of-season recovery threshold
- remaining demand window is too short for normal recovery
- future inbound will worsen seasonal exposure
- high-value or high-volume seasonal SKU is affected
- multiple stores, warehouses, or regions are affected
- warehouse capacity or aging risk is becoming material
- markdown, transfer, or allocation changes may be required
- local action alone is insufficient
- finance, pricing, or executive review may be required
- repeated seasonal overhang pattern suggests structural weakness

## 8. Required Assessment Fields

Every material seasonal inventory exit review should capture, where available:

- seasonal program or campaign reference
- SKU / item ID and description
- affected store, warehouse, or region scope
- seasonal end date or key demand cutoff date
- current on-hand and usable inventory
- projected end-of-season stock position
- expected remaining demand window
- next confirmed inbound or transfer timing
- exit strategy category
- likely cause of exposure
- aging or obsolescence indicator where applicable
- current mitigation options
- priority classification
- response owner
- escalation status
- next review time
- final resolution and outcome

## 9. Standard Exit Strategy Workflow

The normal seasonal inventory exit workflow should follow this sequence:

1. detect the seasonal exposure signal
2. validate inventory, demand-window, and inbound facts
3. classify the exit type and urgency
4. assess business impact and affected scope
5. identify feasible recovery options
6. prioritize the most material seasonal exposure
7. assign response owner
8. communicate to impacted teams
9. escalate if local action is insufficient
10. document final action, decision, and closure

The response should focus on reducing end-of-season exposure efficiently while protecting governance, pricing discipline, and inventory flow.

## 10. Severity Levels

### Level 1 — Local manageable exit case
Exposure exists but can be handled through local adjustment and controlled sell-through.

Examples:
- isolated store-level seasonal excess with moderate value
- one low-priority seasonal SKU with manageable remaining cover
- small inbound reduction opportunity

### Level 2 — Managed operational exit case
Seasonal exit affects important inventory decisions and requires broader visibility.

Examples:
- moderate end-of-season residual stock on important SKU
- regional imbalance in remaining seasonal demand
- future inbound likely to worsen exposure
- targeted markdown review may be needed

### Level 3 — Cross-functional seasonal recovery risk
Exposure materially affects multiple locations, warehouse flow, or value recovery and requires coordinated action.

Examples:
- regional seasonal stock overhang
- warehouse seasonal inventory pressure
- transfer, markdown, and replenishment changes needed together
- repeated end-of-season excess on important category

### Level 4 — Executive or governance seasonal exit case
Exposure creates major financial, operational, or strategic risk and requires exceptional action or approval.

Examples:
- strategic seasonal category with major overhang across regions
- severe warehouse pressure from seasonal inventory
- large-value seasonal clearance decision
- repeated major seasonal exit failure requiring senior review

---

## 11. Business Impact Assessment

Seasonal exit cases should be assessed across the following areas.

### 11.1 Working capital impact
Will remaining seasonal stock tie up material cash beyond the useful demand window?

### 11.2 Warehouse and flow impact
Will seasonal overhang reduce storage flexibility or create congestion?

### 11.3 Commercial recovery impact
Will markdown, transfer, or alternate recovery support be needed?

### 11.4 Inventory quality impact
Will aging, expiry, fashion relevance decline, or obsolescence risk increase if action is delayed?

### 11.5 Governance impact
Does the case require exception approval, executive review, or formal decision logging?

---

## 12. Typical Exit Actions

Depending on the situation, teams may consider:

- reducing or stopping seasonal replenishment
- delaying, reducing, or cancelling future inbound where feasible
- transferring seasonal stock to healthier-demand locations
- initiating controlled markdown or clearance review where approved
- using region-specific exit actions based on local demand decay
- prioritizing warehouse release or containment for pressured stock
- increasing store-level sell-through focus while the demand window remains open
- reviewing alternate channel or outlet options where approved
- increasing monitoring frequency during the final seasonal window
- escalating for controlled policy exception or executive approval

Any material deviation from normal policy must follow approved governance and approval paths.

---

## 13. Prioritization Rules During Seasonal Exit

When recovery capacity is limited, prioritization should generally favor:

1. high-value or high-volume seasonal exposure
2. stock with the shortest remaining relevance window
3. inventory creating warehouse or flow pressure
4. categories with aging, expiry, or obsolescence risk
5. lower-risk seasonal residual stock after higher-exposure inventory is addressed

Priority decisions should be documented when they materially change standard replenishment, transfer, markdown, or commercial behavior.

---

## 14. Communication Standards

When communicating a seasonal inventory exit case, the message should include:

- what seasonal program or item is affected
- affected stores, warehouse, or region
- current seasonal stock exposure
- remaining demand window
- likely cause
- expected business impact
- actions already taken
- action or decision now needed
- owner
- next update time

Avoid vague messages such as:

- “seasonal stock remains”
- “we need to clear this item”
- “season is ending, reduce stock”
- “please review seasonal excess”

Good communication must be clear, specific, and action-oriented.

---

## 15. Escalation Guidance

Escalation should be considered when:

- local recovery action is not enough
- multiple stores or regions are affected
- confirmed inbound will materially worsen exposure
- warehouse pressure or obsolescence risk is becoming serious
- markdown, transfer, or pricing changes require management approval
- finance or commercial leadership alignment is needed
- repeated seasonal overhang pattern suggests structural weakness
- executive approval is needed for exception action

Escalation messages should state:

- issue summary
- affected business scope
- current timing and severity
- actions already taken
- decision or support required
- owner and timing

## 16. Investigation Guidance by Scenario

### 16.1 Controlled sell-through exit
Check:
- remaining seasonal demand strength
- whether stock can normalize without markdown
- whether replenishment has already been reduced enough
- whether the current store mix is still appropriate
- urgency of review if demand declines further

### 16.2 Transfer-supported exit
Check:
- source versus destination demand health
- whether transfer will solve the excess or only move it
- transport feasibility
- timing of remaining seasonal demand at target locations
- expected recovery benefit

### 16.3 Markdown-supported exit
Check:
- remaining seasonal window
- likely markdown effectiveness
- margin trade-off
- whether targeted or broad markdown is needed
- approval path for pricing action

### 16.4 Inbound wind-down exit
Check:
- future inbound still committed
- supplier flexibility
- whether inbound can be delayed, reduced, or stopped
- warehouse impact if inbound continues
- procurement and finance alignment need

### 16.5 Warehouse release exit
Check:
- current warehouse exposure
- storage utilization effect
- ability to redirect or recover inventory through alternate paths
- urgency of flow protection
- whether the stock should be contained or actively pushed out

### 16.6 Structural seasonal exit weakness
Check:
- history of similar seasons
- repeated overhang by category or item type
- forecast and positioning assumptions
- commercial timing weakness
- need for broader governance review

Seasonal inventory exit records should meet these standards:

- valid seasonal program or item reference
- valid SKU and location scope

- known end-of-season timing
- clear exit classification
- owner identity

## 19. Example Decision Messages

### Example A — Controlled sell-through
**Subject:** Review required for end-of-season stock exposure at priority stores

**Message:**  
Stores 118 and 122 retain elevated stock for SKU 44219 as the seasonal demand window nears close. Current sell-through remains positive, but projected end-of-season stock is above the local target if standard flow continues unchanged. Planning should review immediate replenishment wind-down, and regional operations should confirm whether any transfer support is needed by 11:30 AM today.

### Example B — Regional seasonal exit review
**Subject:** Regional seasonal inventory exit strategy review required

**Message:**  
North region shows elevated end-of-season exposure across three seasonal snack SKUs, with future inbound still planned and remaining demand expected to soften next week. Commercial planning, supply chain planning, and regional operations coordination is required today to determine whether transfer, markdown, or inbound wind-down actions are needed.

### Example C — Warehouse seasonal overhang
**Subject:** Warehouse seasonal stock overhang creating exit-strategy risk

**Message:**  
Warehouse W03 continues to hold above-threshold seasonal inventory for a beverage SKU family as the effective selling window narrows. If no action is taken, storage pressure and recovery difficulty are expected to increase materially over the next seven days. Warehouse operations, planning, and pricing teams should review containment and recovery options before 2:00 PM today.

### Example D — Executive escalation
**Subject:** Executive approval required for major seasonal inventory exit action

**Message:**  
A strategic seasonal category now carries major residual stock exposure across two regions as the remaining demand window closes. Standard recovery actions are unlikely to normalize inventory without broader intervention. Planning recommends controlled action across replenishment wind-down, stock rebalancing, and commercial recovery pathways. Executive direction is required before 4:00 PM today.

## 20. Non-Compliant Handling Examples

The following are poor practices:

- waiting until the season fully ends before reviewing visible exit risk
- continuing normal replenishment despite clear end-of-season exposure
- allowing confirmed inbound to worsen avoidable seasonal excess
- changing markdown or transfer behavior without documented reasoning
- failing to inform impacted teams about material seasonal recovery risk
- using vague “season ending” messages without timing or scope
- closing the case without capturing learning for the next seasonal cycle
