## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the standard approach for planning, reviewing, approving, and executing inbound supply resequencing decisions at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that inbound supply is resequenced in a controlled, business-focused, and traceable way when the original inbound plan no longer best supports service, inventory health, warehouse flow, or commercial priorities.

This guide supports the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) by providing governed knowledge for:

- inbound supply resequencing decisions
- inbound prioritization and deprioritization
- service-risk and overstock-risk balancing
- warehouse flow protection
- campaign and event support adjustment
- escalation and approval support
- audit-aware decision traceability

## 2. Scope

This guide applies to inbound supply resequencing situations involving:

- changing inbound arrival priority
- delaying or advancing selected inbound receipts
- reshaping inbound sequence due to warehouse constraints
- reprioritizing inbound by SKU, supplier, or region
- inbound resequencing to protect stockout-risk items
- inbound resequencing to reduce overstock or warehouse pressure
- campaign or seasonal support resequencing
- supplier timing changes requiring revised inbound order
- transport or dock constraints affecting receipt order
- repeated inbound instability requiring structured review

This guide applies to teams such as:

- supply chain planning
- procurement
- warehouse operations
- transport coordination
- regional operations
- store operations
- inventory control
- commercial planning where campaign or strategic items are affected
- finance support where value exposure is relevant
- executive stakeholders for major flow-risk cases

## 3. Definition of Inbound Supply Resequencing

Inbound supply resequencing is the controlled decision to change the planned timing, order, priority, or operational intake sequence of inbound inventory in order to better support current business conditions.

Examples include:

- prioritizing urgent replenishment stock ahead of lower-risk inbound
- delaying non-critical inbound because warehouse capacity is constrained
- advancing selected receipts for high-risk campaign items
- changing dock or receipt order after supplier timing shifts
- resequencing inbound to reduce overstock exposure for slow-moving items
- prioritizing inbound for one region while slowing another based on risk conditions

Inbound resequencing is not only a logistics adjustment.  
It is a planning, service, warehouse-flow, and governance decision.

---

## 4. Core Principles

All inbound supply resequencing actions must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Business priority first
The resequencing decision must support the most important current business need, not local convenience.

### 4.2 Protect service without creating avoidable excess
Inbound should be resequenced to improve service and flow while avoiding unnecessary overstock or congestion.

### 4.3 Use the least disruptive effective change
The business should apply the smallest practical sequence change needed to achieve the objective.

### 4.4 Respect operational feasibility
Resequencing decisions must reflect actual warehouse, transport, supplier, and dock constraints.

### 4.5 Coordinate across functions
Material resequencing often requires planning, procurement, warehouse, transport, and commercial alignment.

### 4.6 Preserve traceability
Reason, owner, timing, impact, approval status, and outcome must be documented appropriately.

## 5. Typical Reasons for Inbound Resequencing

Inbound resequencing may be triggered by:

- emerging stockout risk
- demand spike on selected items
- warehouse capacity constraint
- dock scheduling pressure
- supplier delay on one inbound stream
- supplier early arrival on low-priority stock
- overstock risk on selected items
- promotion or seasonal timing shift
- transfer failure increasing need for direct inbound support
- forecast error affecting short-term priority
- regional imbalance in service risk
- transport disruption
- labor or equipment limitation in receiving
- repeated inbound instability requiring active prioritization

The actual reason should be clearly validated before major resequencing is approved.

---

## 6. Common Resequencing Categories

### 6.1 Service-protection resequencing
Inbound is resequenced to protect high-risk availability needs.

### 6.2 Warehouse-pressure resequencing
Inbound is resequenced to avoid congestion, receiving backlog, or storage overload.

### 6.3 Overstock-avoidance resequencing
Inbound is resequenced to prevent worsening excess inventory exposure.

### 6.4 Campaign-support resequencing
Inbound is resequenced to better support promotion, launch, or seasonal priorities.

### 6.5 Supplier-timing resequencing
Inbound order is changed because supplier dispatch timing or ETA has shifted.

### 6.6 Region-priority resequencing
Inbound is resequenced to support one region or store group ahead of another based on risk.

### 6.7 Structural inbound instability review
Repeated resequencing need suggests broader inbound planning or flow design weakness.

---

## 7. Trigger Conditions

An inbound case should be treated as a formal resequencing review when one or more of the following apply:

- standard inbound sequence will not support required service outcome
- warehouse or dock pressure makes the current receipt plan impractical
- delayed or early inbound materially changes business priority
- high-priority or high-velocity SKU is affected
- campaign, launch, or seasonal timing is exposed
- overstock exposure will worsen if current inbound order continues unchanged
- multiple stores, regions, or warehouses are affected
- local action alone is insufficient
- approval outside normal local authority may be required
- audit traceability is needed because business sensitivity is high

## 8. Required Assessment Fields

Every material inbound resequencing review should capture, where available:

- resequencing request ID or reference
- supplier ID / supplier name
- purchase order, shipment, or inbound reference
- SKU / item ID and description
- affected warehouse, store, or region scope
- original inbound sequence or timing
- proposed revised sequence or timing
- resequencing category
- business reason
- current service or inventory risk
- warehouse or dock impact
- expected start date and end date
- expected business benefit
- approval owner
- escalation status
- next review time
- final decision and outcome

## 9. Standard Resequencing Workflow

The normal inbound resequencing workflow should follow this sequence:

1. identify the need for inbound sequence change
2. validate the operational and business facts
3. classify the resequencing type and urgency
4. assess scope, risk, and expected impact
5. determine whether local discretion is sufficient or approval is required
6. assign resequencing owner
7. communicate to impacted teams where needed
8. approve, reject, or modify the resequencing request
9. monitor the new inbound sequence while active
10. document closure and lessons if needed

The workflow should protect business agility while maintaining inbound control and governance.

## 10. Severity Levels

### Level 1 — Local controlled resequencing
The change is limited in scope and can be handled within defined local authority.

Examples:
- small dock-order adjustment for one low-risk inbound
- minor timing change with minimal wider impact
- isolated receiving reprioritization inside one warehouse shift

### Level 2 — Managed operational resequencing
The change affects important items or multiple inbound flows and requires broader review or visibility.

Examples:
- service-driven reprioritization for important SKU
- temporary inbound delay on low-priority stock due to warehouse pressure
- moderate campaign-support inbound advancement
- selected region-priority change

### Level 3 — Cross-functional resequencing case
The resequencing materially affects multiple teams, service outcomes, warehouse flow, or inventory exposure and requires coordinated approval.

Examples:
- warehouse and planning jointly reshaping inbound flow
- urgent inbound reprioritization across multiple suppliers
- stockout-risk protection requiring inbound sequence override
- overstock-avoidance resequencing affecting many SKUs

### Level 4 — Executive or governance resequencing case
The resequencing creates major financial, operational, or strategic exposure and requires senior approval.

Examples:
- strategic SKU family inbound reprioritized across regions
- major warehouse recovery plan requiring broad inbound reshaping
- campaign-critical resequencing with meaningful commercial exposure
- repeated large-scale resequencing indicating structural inbound planning weakness

## 11. Business Impact Assessment

Inbound resequencing cases should be assessed across the following areas.

### 11.1 Service impact
Will the revised sequence better protect availability and reduce stockout exposure?

### 11.2 Inventory impact
Will the revised sequence reduce avoidable overstock, imbalance, or aging risk?

### 11.3 Warehouse and receiving impact
Will the revised sequence improve or worsen receiving flow, dock pressure, and storage efficiency?

### 11.4 Commercial impact
Will the revised sequence support campaign, launch, seasonal, or strategic item priorities?

### 11.5 Governance impact
Does the case require exception approval, executive review, or formal decision logging?

---

## 12. Evaluation Questions

Before approving inbound resequencing, teams should ask:

- What will happen if the current inbound sequence stays unchanged?
- What business problem is the resequencing solving?
- Is the change temporary or likely to repeat?
- What is the smallest workable sequence adjustment?
- Which items, suppliers, regions, or warehouses are affected?
- Will the change improve service, reduce excess, or relieve warehouse pressure?
- What disruption could the revised sequence create elsewhere?
- Does the change require supplier, transport, or dock coordination?
- Who must approve it?
- How will the revised sequence be monitored and closed?

These questions help prevent unnecessary or weakly justified inbound changes.

## 13. Typical Response Actions

Depending on the case, teams may consider:

- advancing critical inbound receipt timing where feasible
- delaying non-critical inbound intake
- changing receipt order by SKU or supplier priority
- adjusting dock scheduling sequence
- protecting warehouse receiving capacity for urgent items
- coordinating revised supplier delivery windows
- reducing intake of low-priority or excess-prone stock
- supporting one region ahead of another under controlled policy
- increasing monitoring of the revised inbound plan
- escalating for higher approval where service or financial exposure is material

Any material deviation from standard inbound flow must follow approved governance and approval paths.

---

## 14. Prioritization Rules During Resequencing

When inbound flexibility is limited, prioritization should generally favor:

1. service-critical and high-velocity SKUs
2. items or regions facing imminent stockout risk
3. campaign, launch, or seasonal support inventory with narrow timing windows
4. inbound changes that reduce serious warehouse or overstock pressure
5. lower-risk inbound adjustments after higher-exposure cases are addressed

Priority decisions should be documented when they materially change standard inbound behavior.

---

## 15. Communication Standards

When communicating an inbound resequencing case, the message should include:

- what inbound flow or shipment is affected
- original versus proposed sequence or timing
- affected item and scope
- business reason
- expected duration
- expected service, inventory, or warehouse impact
- actions already reviewed
- decision or approval needed
- owner
- next update time if active

Avoid vague messages such as:

- “change the inbound order”
- “receive this later”
- “bring this first”
- “urgent inbound change needed”

Good communication must be clear, specific, and action-oriented.

---

## 16. Escalation Guidance

Escalation should be considered when:

- the resequencing exceeds local authority
- multiple suppliers, warehouses, stores, or regions are affected
- warehouse, procurement, and planning all need coordinated alignment
- the change materially affects campaign or strategic item support
- service or financial exposure is meaningful
- repeated resequencing use indicates structural weakness
- executive approval is needed for the requested deviation

Escalation messages should state:

- issue summary
- affected business scope
- proposed inbound sequence change
- why the standard sequence is not sufficient
- actions already taken
- decision or support required
- owner and timing

## 17. Investigation Guidance by Scenario

### 17.1 Service-protection resequencing
Check:
- current stockout-risk items
- current and projected cover
- whether inbound timing can realistically improve service
- impact on deprioritized stock
- urgency of decision

### 17.2 Warehouse-pressure resequencing
Check:
- receiving backlog
- dock pressure
- storage utilization
- labor and equipment availability
- whether delaying low-priority inbound protects overall flow

### 17.3 Overstock-avoidance resequencing
Check:
- current excess exposure
- confirmed future inbound still in flow
- whether delay or resequencing will reduce avoidable stock build
- supplier flexibility
- downstream warehouse effect

### 17.4 Campaign-support resequencing
Check:
- campaign or launch timing
- required inventory positioning date
- store or regional exposure
- whether current inbound sequence is too late or misaligned
- commercial approval path if timing is critical

### 17.5 Supplier-timing resequencing
Check:
- revised dispatch or ETA detail
- whether early or late arrival changes business priority
- available dock capacity
- alternate receipt slot options
- planning and warehouse coordination needs

### 17.6 Structural resequencing weakness
Check:
- history of repeated inbound changes
- common supplier or warehouse pattern
- planning or slotting weakness
- whether a broader flow redesign is needed
- need for governance review

## 19. Data Quality and Traceability Expectations

Inbound resequencing records should meet these standards:

- valid resequencing reference
- valid supplier and shipment scope
- usable timestamp
- clear original-versus-revised sequence description
- clear reason and expected duration
- owner identity
- approval status
- next review time for active case
- expected impact note

Poor-quality resequencing records reduce governance quality and weaken trust in inbound decision support.

## 20. Example Decision Messages

### Example A — Service-protection resequencing
**Subject:** Approval required for inbound resequencing to protect high-risk availability

**Message:**  
Inbound receipt priority is requested for SKU 44219 shipments arriving at Warehouse W03 because current stock cover in five West region stores is below the required protection threshold. Under the existing receipt order, lower-risk inventory would be processed first and service recovery would be delayed. Warehouse operations and planning have reviewed feasibility, and approval is required by 1:00 PM today.

### Example B — Warehouse-pressure resequencing
**Subject:** Temporary inbound resequencing required due to receiving pressure

**Message:**  
Warehouse W03 is currently operating under elevated receiving congestion, and the planned inbound sequence for tomorrow includes several low-priority shipments that may worsen dock pressure without supporting urgent business needs. A temporary resequencing is requested so that urgent replenishment stock can be processed first while slower-moving inbound is delayed within approved limits. Warehouse and procurement alignment is needed today.

### Example C — Overstock-avoidance resequencing
**Subject:** Inbound resequencing review needed to reduce excess inventory exposure

**Message:**  
West region beverage inventory is already above target cover, and the next two inbound receipts include low-priority stock that will worsen excess exposure if processed on the current plan. Planning requests review of a revised inbound sequence that delays selected receipts and prioritizes healthier-demand items first. Procurement and warehouse operations should confirm feasibility before 2:00 PM today.

### Example D — Executive escalation
**Subject:** Executive approval required for major inbound supply resequencing action

**Message:**  
A strategic SKU family across two regions now requires controlled inbound resequencing due to combined stockout risk on priority items and warehouse pressure from lower-priority inbound. Standard receipt sequencing is no longer sufficient to protect service and flow. Planning recommends temporary coordinated reshaping of inbound priorities across suppliers and warehouses. Executive direction is required before 4:00 PM today.

## 21. Non-Compliant Handling Examples

The following are poor practices:

- changing inbound order with no recorded business reason
- prioritizing receipts based only on local convenience
- repeatedly reshaping inbound without structural review
- failing to state duration or expected impact of the new sequence
- not informing impacted teams about material inbound change
- documenting only “warehouse request” without business context
- approving major inbound resequencing without proper authority
