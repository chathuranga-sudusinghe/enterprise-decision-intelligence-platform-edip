## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the standard operational response for supplier delay situations at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that supplier delays are identified early, assessed consistently, communicated clearly, and managed in a business-focused way before they cause broader disruption across replenishment, warehouse flow, store availability, promotion readiness, and regional operations.

This guide supports the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) by providing governed knowledge for:

- supplier delay detection and response
- ETA risk handling
- replenishment disruption review
- mitigation planning
- escalation and approval support
- audit-aware operational reasoning

---

## 2. Scope

This guide applies to supplier delay situations involving:

- purchase order delays
- inbound shipment delays
- partial shipment delays
- ASN or shipping confirmation slippage
- supplier production delays
- carrier pickup delays linked to supplier handoff
- customs or documentation delay affecting inbound flow
- repeated supplier lateness patterns
- supplier short-ship events with material timing impact

This guide applies to teams such as:

- procurement
- supply chain planning
- warehouse operations
- transport coordination
- regional operations
- store operations
- inventory control
- commercial teams where promotion or launch activity is affected
- executive stakeholders for major disruptions

---

## 3. Definition of Supplier Delay

A supplier delay exists when a supplier fails, or is expected to fail, to meet the required shipment, dispatch, delivery, or availability timing needed to support the agreed replenishment or business plan.

Examples include:

- supplier confirms a revised ETA later than required
- shipment remains unconfirmed beyond the expected dispatch window
- only part of the planned quantity ships on time
- inbound arrival will miss the required warehouse receiving slot
- promotion-linked inventory will not arrive before execution deadline
- repeated lateness creates ongoing service risk

A supplier delay is not only a procurement issue.  
It is an operational and business continuity risk with downstream effects.

---

## 4. Core Principles

All supplier delay responses must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Confirm facts quickly
The business must validate the delay signal, revised ETA, affected quantity, and risk window as early as possible.

### 4.2 Focus on business impact
The response must assess how the delay affects inventory availability, replenishment flow, campaigns, and service commitments.

### 4.3 Protect critical supply first
When delay risk exists, mitigation should prioritize service-critical items, high-velocity SKUs, and time-sensitive demand windows.

### 4.4 Escalate before service failure
Material supplier delays should be escalated early enough to support mitigation before store or warehouse impact becomes severe.

### 4.5 Use one coordination owner
Each material delay case should have one clearly identified response owner.

### 4.6 Preserve traceability
Major supplier delay decisions, exception actions, and approvals must be documented appropriately.

## 5. Typical Causes of Supplier Delay

Supplier delays may arise from:

- supplier production shortfall
- raw material shortage
- labor shortage at supplier site
- transport pickup delay
- customs or compliance hold
- documentation error
- packaging or labeling issue
- quality hold before dispatch
- weather or route disruption
- carrier capacity shortage
- incorrect forecast or order timing assumptions
- supplier prioritization conflict
- recurring supplier performance weakness

The actual cause should be validated before assigning responsibility or deciding major corrective action.

---

## 6. Common Delay Categories

### 6.1 Confirmed dispatch delay
Supplier confirms that shipment dispatch will occur later than planned.

### 6.2 Delivery ETA delay
Goods have shipped, but arrival timing is later than the required operational window.

### 6.3 Partial shipment delay
Only part of the planned quantity is shipped or expected on time.

### 6.4 Unconfirmed shipment risk
Expected shipment remains unconfirmed close to the required dispatch deadline.

### 6.5 Promotion-critical supplier delay
Delay affects inventory linked to approved campaign, launch, or seasonal activity.

### 6.6 Repeated supplier lateness
Delay pattern shows recurring service-level weakness rather than isolated disruption.

### 6.7 Documentation or compliance delay
Paperwork, customs, or regulatory issue delays physical movement or receipt.

---

## 7. Trigger Conditions

A supplier delay should be treated as a formal response case when one or more of the following apply:

- revised ETA misses required replenishment window
- shipment confirmation is missing beyond agreed checkpoint
- delay affects high-priority or high-velocity items
- delay affects active promotion or seasonal activity
- multiple stores, regions, or warehouses may be impacted
- available cover is insufficient until revised arrival
- supplier has repeated recent lateness
- warehouse receiving or transport plans need adjustment
- local procurement action alone is insufficient
- executive or policy exception may be required

---

## 8. Required Assessment Fields

Every material supplier delay review should capture, where available:

- supplier ID / supplier name
- purchase order or shipment reference
- affected SKU / SKU group
- affected quantity
- original required date
- revised ETA or current uncertainty status
- delay category
- likely cause
- current available cover
- affected warehouse, store, or region scope
- promotion or campaign relevance
- mitigation options considered
- current response owner
- escalation status
- next review time
- final resolution

## 9. Standard Response Workflow

The normal response workflow should follow this sequence:

1. detect the supplier delay signal
2. validate shipment facts and revised ETA
3. classify the delay type and urgency
4. assess inventory, service, and commercial impact
5. identify available mitigation options
6. assign response owner
7. communicate to impacted teams
8. escalate if risk exceeds local control
9. confirm action path and timing
10. document closure and lessons where needed

The response should aim to protect continuity while preserving operational control and traceability.

## 10. Severity Levels

### Level 1 — Local manageable delay
Delay exists but can be handled by procurement or local planning with minimal wider impact.

Examples:
- low-risk item with sufficient cover
- short delay with no campaign relevance
- isolated PO timing issue with available buffer

### Level 2 — Managed operational delay
Delay affects replenishment planning or warehouse coordination and requires cross-team visibility.

Examples:
- moderate delay on important SKU
- partial shipment requiring replanning
- repeated unconfirmed dispatch risk
- reduced cover for selected locations

### Level 3 — Cross-functional supply disruption
Delay materially affects service, warehouse flow, promotions, or regional operations.

Examples:
- supplier delay affecting multiple stores or regions
- low-cover situation for high-velocity items
- delay requiring transfer, allocation, or reprioritization
- campaign inventory missing required arrival window

### Level 4 — Executive or governance escalation
Delay creates major business risk or requires exception approval.

Examples:
- major shortage risk ahead of key trading window
- supplier failure affecting strategic item group
- large promotion support risk
- urgent allocation or policy exception required

---

## 11. Business Impact Assessment

Supplier delay cases should be assessed across the following areas.

### 11.1 Service impact
Will the delay reduce store availability, warehouse readiness, or delivery continuity?

### 11.2 Inventory cover impact
How long can current stock support demand before the revised arrival date?

### 11.3 Commercial impact
Does the delay threaten promotion execution, launch timing, or revenue-critical activity?

### 11.4 Operational impact
Will warehouse, transport, or store execution plans need to change?

### 11.5 Governance impact
Does the issue require controlled exception, approval, or executive review?

---

## 12. Immediate Mitigation Options

Depending on the situation, teams may consider:

- confirming revised ETA and shipment confidence immediately
- reprioritizing existing available stock
- initiating inter-location transfer review
- adjusting replenishment quantities temporarily
- protecting service-critical stores first
- delaying lower-priority allocations
- sourcing alternate supply where approved
- coordinating emergency or expedited transport where justified
- adjusting promotion execution plans if needed
- increasing monitoring frequency until risk stabilizes
- escalating to supplier management or executive review

Any material exception to normal policy must follow approved governance and approval paths.

---

## 13. Prioritization Rules During Delay

When supply is constrained due to supplier delay, prioritization should generally favor:

1. service-critical and high-velocity SKUs
2. stores or regions facing imminent stockout risk
3. active promotion or launch support items
4. contractually or strategically important demand commitments
5. normal lower-priority replenishment after high-risk needs are protected

Priority decisions should be documented when they materially change standard distribution or replenishment behavior.

---

## 14. Communication Standards

When communicating a supplier delay, the message should include:

- what is delayed
- supplier and shipment reference
- original required timing and revised ETA
- affected quantity and scope
- expected business impact
- actions already taken
- action or decision now needed
- owner
- next update time

Avoid vague messages such as:

- “supplier is late”
- “shipment may not arrive”
- “there is some delay”
- “please check urgently”

Good communication must be clear, specific, and action-oriented.

---

## 15. Escalation Guidance

Escalation should be considered when:

- revised ETA threatens service-critical availability
- multiple stores, regions, or warehouses are affected
- procurement cannot secure a reliable recovery plan quickly
- available cover is insufficient
- campaign or launch timing is at risk
- warehouse and planning need coordinated mitigation
- delay pattern suggests broader supplier performance weakness
- executive approval is needed for exception response

Escalation messages should state:

- issue summary
- affected business scope
- current risk and timing
- actions already taken
- decision or support required
- owner and timing

## 16. Investigation Guidance by Scenario

### 16.1 Confirmed dispatch delay
Check:
- supplier confirmation timestamp
- revised dispatch date
- cause provided by supplier
- quantity affected
- whether partial shipment is possible
- current inventory cover

### 16.2 Unconfirmed shipment risk
Check:
- PO status
- shipment booking status
- required dispatch cutoff
- supplier response quality
- whether issue is recurring
- urgency of escalation

### 16.3 Delivery ETA delay
Check:
- carrier status
- route disruption
- customs or documentation issue
- revised warehouse arrival slot impact
- store service effect if inbound timing shifts

### 16.4 Promotion-critical delay
Check:
- promotion start date
- required inventory positioning date
- affected stores or regions
- available substitute or fallback option
- commercial communication need
- executive decision need if launch is at risk

### 16.5 Repeated supplier lateness
Check:
- recent supplier fill-rate and on-time pattern
- number of repeated incidents
- affected categories or SKUs
- escalation history
- need for supplier performance review

## 17. Link to EDIP Decisioning

Supplier delays matter to EDIP because they can affect:

- replenishment recommendations
- transfer suggestions
- regional inventory risk scoring
- planner override decisions
- warehouse capacity response
- promotion readiness assessment
- executive exception decisioning

For this reason, material supplier delay events should be visible to relevant planning, procurement, operations, and governance functions when they alter downstream decisions.

## 18. Data Quality and Traceability Expectations

Supplier delay records should meet these standards:

- valid supplier reference
- valid PO or shipment reference
- clear affected SKU or quantity scope
- valid original date and revised ETA where available
- usable delay classification
- clear owner identity
- next review time for active case
- mitigation or escalation note
- final resolution captured for material cases

Poor-quality supplier delay records reduce response quality and weaken operational control.

## 19. Example Response Messages

### Example A — Confirmed dispatch delay
**Subject:** Supplier dispatch delay requires replenishment review

**Message:**  
Supplier S-184 has confirmed a two-day dispatch delay for PO 45009182 covering three beverage SKUs planned for West Distribution Center. Current cover is sufficient for one SKU but not for the remaining two if revised timing holds. Procurement has requested confirmation on partial shipment feasibility. Planning should review transfer and allocation options by 1:00 PM today.

### Example B — Unconfirmed shipment risk
**Subject:** Urgent follow-up required for unconfirmed supplier shipment

**Message:**  
The expected shipment for PO 45010311 from Supplier S-247 remains unconfirmed beyond the required dispatch checkpoint. The order supports replenishment for six stores with below-target cover on a high-velocity item group. Procurement is awaiting final supplier confirmation. Regional planning and inventory control should prepare contingency options if confirmation is not received by 12:30 PM.

### Example C — Promotion-critical delay
**Subject:** Campaign inventory at risk due to supplier ETA delay

**Message:**  
A revised ETA from Supplier S-066 indicates that promotional stock for the approved weekend snack campaign may arrive after the required warehouse processing window. This creates execution risk for launch-day store readiness. Procurement has requested expedited handling options, and commercial coordination may be needed if timing cannot be recovered by end of day.

### Example D — Executive escalation
**Subject:** Executive approval required for supply exception due to major supplier delay

**Message:**  
A major supplier delay now affects a strategic SKU family across two regions ahead of a high-volume trading period. Current inventory cover is insufficient to support standard replenishment flow until the revised ETA. Planning recommends controlled allocation and temporary exception to normal distribution priorities. Executive direction is required before 4:00 PM today.

---

## 20. Non-Compliant Response Examples

The following are poor practices:

- waiting for stockout before escalating a material delay
- failing to confirm revised ETA or shipment confidence
- not informing planning when low cover is visible
- making major priority changes without documented reasoning
- sending vague supplier delay messages without scope or timing
- ignoring repeated lateness pattern
- treating promotion-linked delay as routine inbound noise
