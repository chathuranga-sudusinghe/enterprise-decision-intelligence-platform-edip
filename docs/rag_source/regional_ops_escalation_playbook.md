## 1. Purpose

This playbook defines the standard escalation approach for regional operational issues across NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that regional issues are escalated in a consistent, timely, and business-focused way so that cross-functional teams can respond effectively before service, inventory, or commercial performance deteriorates further.

This playbook supports the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) by giving governed escalation guidance that can be used for:

- regional exception handling
- cross-store issue response
- supply and replenishment risk escalation
- promotion and pricing execution escalation
- warehouse and inbound disruption response
- executive decision support
- audit-friendly operational reasoning

---

## 2. Scope

This playbook applies to regional issues involving:

- inventory availability risk
- repeated store execution failures
- replenishment breakdowns
- supplier service disruption
- inbound shipment delays
- warehouse processing bottlenecks
- pricing or promotion execution risk
- regional service level deterioration
- repeated planner override patterns
- unresolved cross-functional operational issues

This playbook applies to communication and action coordination between:

- regional operations teams
- store operations leaders
- warehouse operations
- supply chain planning
- procurement
- merchandising
- pricing teams
- data and analytics support
- finance support where required
- executive leadership for high-impact cases

## 3. What Qualifies as a Regional Escalation

A regional escalation is required when an issue is no longer isolated to a single local owner or store and instead creates broader risk that needs cross-functional coordination, managerial intervention, or executive visibility.

A regional escalation may be triggered when:

- multiple stores are affected
- one warehouse disruption impacts a region
- a repeated issue shows systemic failure
- local corrective action failed
- customer service or sales impact is material
- a policy exception or approval is required
- the issue crosses planning, store, warehouse, and supplier boundaries

Regional escalation is not for routine daily noise. It is for material issues needing structured intervention.

---

## 4. Escalation Principles

All regional escalations must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Escalate with facts
Escalations must be based on confirmed or sufficiently validated facts, not assumption or blame.

### 4.2 Focus on business impact
The message must clearly explain why the issue matters in operational or commercial terms.

### 4.3 Use one owner
Each escalation must have one clearly identified coordination owner.

### 4.4 Show what has already been tried
Escalations should not start at the top without showing prior checks or attempted corrective action where time allows.

### 4.5 Match severity to audience
The escalation level must fit the actual risk level.

### 4.6 Support action, not just awareness
Every escalation must ask for a clear next action, decision, or support requirement.

### 4.7 Preserve traceability
Material escalations must be documented in controlled records or systems when they affect formal decisions or approvals.

## 5. Common Regional Escalation Scenarios

Regional operations should use this playbook for scenarios such as:

- repeated shelf stockouts across multiple stores despite deliveries
- regional warehouse receiving backlog affecting store availability
- supplier delay causing likely stockout in a region
- active promotion not executed correctly across store cluster
- price mismatch affecting multiple locations
- regional demand spike outpacing forecast and replenishment timing
- repeated override trend suggesting planning instability
- transfer execution delay affecting high-priority store set
- regional service level drop against target
- local issue becoming region-wide customer experience risk

## 6. Escalation Levels

NRD uses four escalation levels for regional operational control.

### Level 1 — Local operational handling
The issue can still be managed by store, local warehouse, or direct functional owner without regional escalation.

Examples:
- one store shelf issue
- one delayed display setup
- isolated inventory discrepancy
- minor label correction

### Level 2 — Regional coordination escalation
The issue affects more than one local unit or requires regional coordination.

Examples:
- same issue across several stores
- inbound delay affecting a regional delivery wave
- transfer issue involving multiple stores
- repeated local failure requiring regional intervention

### Level 3 — Cross-functional management escalation
The issue needs coordinated action from multiple functions and may affect service, inventory health, or promotion success materially.

Examples:
- warehouse bottleneck plus supplier delay
- promotion risk combined with pricing and stock issues
- persistent stock availability gap across top stores
- repeated override pattern with service risk

### Level 4 — Executive escalation
The issue has major business impact, requires policy exception, or needs executive direction.

Examples:
- major regional shortage before key trading window
- large promotion failure across important stores
- region-wide supply disruption
- major financial exposure from operational failure
- urgent allocation or exception approval needed

---

## 7. Escalation Trigger Criteria

Regional escalation should be considered when one or more of the following apply:

- multiple stores affected by the same issue
- service level risk exceeds acceptable threshold
- projected stockout for high-priority SKU group
- warehouse processing delay threatens delivery cycle
- supplier ETA risk impacts committed availability
- promotion launch or active campaign is at risk
- issue remains unresolved beyond agreed time window
- repeated exception pattern is visible
- local owner lacks authority to resolve the issue
- action requires cross-functional approval

The trigger should be recorded clearly in the escalation summary.

---

## 8. Required Escalation Message Structure

Every material regional escalation should follow this structure.

### 8.1 Situation
State what happened.

### 8.2 Affected scope
State the region, stores, warehouse, SKUs, campaign, supplier, or process area affected.

### 8.3 Business impact
State why the issue matters now.

Examples:
- lost sales risk
- stockout exposure
- promotion failure risk
- service level deterioration
- pricing compliance concern
- customer experience impact

### 8.4 Actions already taken
State what checks, contacts, or corrective steps have already occurred.

### 8.5 Decision or support needed
State what the escalation is asking for.

### 8.6 Owner and timing
State who owns coordination and when the next update or decision is required.

---

## 9. Standard Escalation Workflow

The normal regional escalation flow should follow this order.

1. identify the issue
2. validate the basic facts
3. assess scale and severity
4. assign a regional coordination owner
5. notify the right audience level
6. request required action or decision
7. track status until closure
8. confirm resolution or escalation outcome
9. log final decision where governance requires it

Escalation should be progressive unless the severity is obviously high enough to require immediate senior attention.

## 10. Roles and Responsibilities

### 10.1 Regional operations lead
Primary coordinator for regional escalations involving store or execution impact.

Responsibilities:
- assess regional severity
- coordinate follow-up across local owners
- maintain issue visibility
- escalate when local control is insufficient
- confirm closure status

### 10.2 Store operations manager
Responsible for validating and addressing store-level facts and execution gaps.

### 10.3 Warehouse operations lead
Responsible for issues involving receiving, dispatch, transfer preparation, or capacity bottlenecks.

### 10.4 Supply chain planning lead
Responsible when the issue affects replenishment logic, forecast response, allocation, or override decisions.

### 10.5 Procurement lead
Responsible for supplier contact, ETA confirmation, and external supply escalation.

### 10.6 Pricing / merchandising lead
Responsible for promotion setup, pricing alignment, display execution, and commercial compliance issues.

### 10.7 Executive sponsor
Provides final direction for high-impact, policy-exception, or enterprise-priority decisions.

## 11. Escalation by Scenario Type

### 11.1 Inventory availability escalation
Use when product availability risk is material across the region.

The escalation should include:
- affected SKUs
- affected stores
- current and projected cover
- supply or execution cause
- immediate recommendation
- timing of risk window

### 11.2 Warehouse disruption escalation
Use when receiving, put-away, or dispatch delay affects regional service.

Include:
- warehouse ID or name
- impacted stores or lanes
- expected operational delay
- current backlog condition
- mitigation options
- decision need if capacity reallocation is required

### 11.3 Supplier delay escalation
Use when supplier service disruption affects replenishment timing materially.

Include:
- supplier name / supplier ID
- affected item group
- revised ETA or uncertainty level
- current regional inventory exposure
- recommended mitigation
- procurement action status

### 11.4 Promotion execution escalation
Use when an approved campaign is not being executed correctly across regional scope.

Include:
- campaign name
- launch date or active period
- affected stores
- missing or failed execution element
- revenue or customer impact risk
- correction deadline

### 11.5 Pricing compliance escalation
Use when pricing mismatch affects multiple stores or creates brand / customer risk.

Include:
- affected item(s)
- affected store count
- approved price vs observed price
- active campaign relevance
- correction owner
- timing requirement

### 11.6 Repeated override pattern escalation
Use when planner overrides show repeated patterns suggesting forecast, replenishment, or governance concern.

Include:
- override type trend
- affected products / locations
- business impact pattern
- whether overrides improved or worsened outcomes
- whether policy review is needed

## 12. Severity Assessment Guidance

Regional teams should consider these severity factors:

- number of stores affected
- importance of affected SKUs
- expected customer impact
- promotion or seasonal relevance
- duration of disruption
- whether the issue is getting worse
- whether a key policy or target is at risk
- whether executive approval is required

A small issue in a normal week may be manageable.  
The same issue during a major campaign or low-cover period may require urgent escalation.

---

## 13. Time Expectations

Escalations should include a time expectation based on severity.

### Critical
Immediate communication and same-day update cycle.

### High
Same-day communication with clear action owner and next review time.

### Standard
Structured follow-up within normal operational review cadence.

If timing is not stated, the escalation is incomplete.

---

## 14. Cross-Functional Coordination Rules

When a regional issue involves multiple teams:

- assign one escalation owner
- keep one shared issue summary
- avoid conflicting instructions
- confirm who owns each action
- provide update checkpoints
- close the issue formally once resolved

Cross-functional escalation should create clarity, not noise.

---

## 15. Escalation Communication Style

Regional escalation messages must be:

- concise
- factual
- business-focused
- respectful
- solution-oriented

Recommended style:
- describe the issue
- explain the impact
- show what has been checked
- state what decision or help is needed
- assign owner and timing

Avoid:
- blame statements
- vague urgency
- unsupported speculation
- long unstructured threads
- unclear ownership

## 16. Example Escalation Messages

### Example A — Regional inventory risk
**Subject:** Regional coordination required for projected stockout risk in West stores

**Message:**  
West region shows projected stockout risk for five high-velocity beverage SKUs across eight stores before the next planned replenishment window. Current review suggests the issue is driven by supplier ETA slippage plus lower-than-expected available cover. Procurement has requested updated supplier confirmation, and planning is reviewing transfer options. Regional coordination is required now to align stores, warehouse, and planning on mitigation actions before 3:00 PM today.

### Example B — Warehouse disruption
**Subject:** Regional escalation for receiving backlog affecting South store deliveries

**Message:**  
South Regional Warehouse is currently operating with a receiving backlog that is delaying store dispatch preparation for the next outbound cycle. Twelve stores may be affected if backlog clearance does not improve by mid-day. Warehouse operations has begun reprioritization, but support is needed to confirm whether non-critical inbound can be deferred temporarily. Regional operations to coordinate next update at 1:00 PM.

### Example C — Promotion execution failure
**Subject:** Cross-functional escalation required for incomplete campaign launch execution

**Message:**  
A weekend promotion has not been fully executed across six stores in the North region. Missing display materials and pricing inconsistencies have been confirmed at store level. This creates immediate launch-day sales and compliance risk. Merchandising, pricing, and regional store operations support are required to complete corrections before 2:00 PM today.

### Example D — Executive escalation
**Subject:** Executive approval required for emergency allocation decision

**Message:**  
A supplier disruption now affects a high-priority SKU family across two regions ahead of a key trading period. Current inventory position is insufficient to support normal replenishment flow. Planning recommends controlled allocation and temporary exception to standard regional distribution rules. Executive direction is required by 4:30 PM to avoid major service impact.

---

## 17. Resolution and Closure Rules

An escalation is not complete until the coordination owner confirms one of the following:

- issue resolved
- issue stabilized with temporary control
- issue moved to higher escalation level
- decision approved and executed
- issue logged for formal follow-up review

Closure communication should include:
- final status
- action taken
- unresolved residual risk if any
- next monitoring requirement if needed

---

## 18. Link to Governance and Decision Trace

Material regional escalations should align with:

- executive exception escalation policy
- audit logging and decision trace policy
- internal operational communication guidelines
- approval authority matrix
- relevant inventory, pricing, promotion, and override governance documents

If the escalation changes a formal replenishment, allocation, pricing, or operational decision, the final decision must be reflected in the proper controlled record.
