## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the standard response approach for overstock risk situations at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that emerging overstock risk is identified early, assessed consistently, communicated clearly, and managed in a controlled, business-focused way before excess inventory creates avoidable working capital pressure, markdown exposure, warehouse congestion, poor flow efficiency, or write-off risk.

This guide supports the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) by providing governed knowledge for:

- overstock risk detection
- inventory excess review
- replenishment and inbound adjustment
- transfer and markdown support decisions
- warehouse capacity protection
- escalation and approval support
- audit-aware decision traceability

## 2. Scope

This guide applies to overstock risk situations involving:

- store-level excess inventory risk
- warehouse-level overstock exposure

- forecast overestimation creating excess stock

- campaign or seasonal inventory left above expected need
- slow-moving stock accumulation

This guide applies to teams such as:

- procurement

## 3. Definition of Overstock Risk

An overstock risk exists when current inventory, confirmed inbound supply, or planned replenishment flow is greater than what is reasonably needed to support expected demand within the relevant service and review window.

Examples include:

- projected days of cover rise materially above target
- future inbound will arrive after demand has slowed
- campaign stock remains high after promotional demand fades
- forecast overestimation leads to excess replenishment
- warehouse inventory builds faster than outbound movement
- slow-moving stock occupies capacity needed for healthier flow
- repeated replenishment or transfer decisions increase excess without business justification

An overstock risk is not only an inventory quantity issue.  
It is a working capital, flow efficiency, and operational control risk.

---

## 4. Core Principles

All overstock risk responses must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Act before excess becomes chronic
Overstock risk should be addressed before excess inventory becomes operationally difficult or financially costly to correct.

### 4.2 Focus on business impact
Response actions must consider working capital, markdown exposure, warehouse pressure, service trade-offs, and product life-cycle realities.

### 4.3 Protect inventory quality and flow
When excess exists, response should aim to improve healthy inventory movement without creating unnecessary service damage elsewhere.

### 4.4 Use validated facts
Decisions should be based on current on-hand stock, usable inventory, demand outlook, confirmed inbound timing, and realistic sell-through potential.

### 4.5 Coordinate across functions
Material overstock risk often requires planning, commercial, warehouse, procurement, and finance-aware coordination.

### 4.6 Preserve traceability
Major mitigation, inbound adjustment, transfer, markdown, or escalation decisions must be documented appropriately.

## 5. Typical Causes of Overstock Risk

Overstock risk may arise from:

- forecast overestimation
- promotion underperformance
- supplier over-delivery or early delivery
- demand slowdown
- seasonal demand shift
- product life-cycle decline
- transfer imbalance
- replenishment policy set too aggressively
- low-confidence forecast handled without sufficient caution
- store execution weakness reducing sell-through
- warehouse flow distortion
- delayed markdown decision-making
- repeated manual override behavior
- item substitution or assortment change
- inbound timing misaligned with actual demand

The actual cause should be validated before major corrective action is taken.

---

## 6. Common Overstock Risk Categories

### 6.1 Store-level overstock risk
One or more stores hold materially more stock than expected demand can absorb within the review window.

### 6.2 Warehouse overstock risk
Warehouse stock levels exceed healthy flow needs and may create capacity or aging concerns.

### 6.3 Regional overstock risk
Multiple stores or a full region hold excess inventory for the same SKU or category.

### 6.4 Forecast-linked overstock risk
Excess inventory results from forecast overestimation or weak short-term demand interpretation.

### 6.5 Promotion or seasonal residual overstock risk
Inventory positioned for campaign or seasonal demand remains high after expected demand fails to materialize.

### 6.6 Slow-moving inventory risk
Stock movement is materially below plan, increasing aging and excess exposure.

### 6.7 Inbound-driven overstock risk
Confirmed or planned inbound supply will materially worsen already elevated inventory exposure.

---

## 7. Trigger Conditions

An overstock risk should be treated as a formal response case when one or more of the following apply:

- projected cover exceeds local excess threshold
- confirmed inbound will worsen already elevated stock position
- high-value or high-volume SKU is affected
- warehouse capacity or storage efficiency is at risk
- campaign or seasonal stock remains above expected need
- slow-moving or aging stock pattern is visible
- multiple stores or a region are affected
- local action alone is insufficient
- finance, commercial, or executive review may be required
- repeated excess pattern suggests structural planning weakness

## 8. Required Assessment Fields

Every material overstock risk review should capture, where available:

- SKU / item ID and description
- affected store, warehouse, or region scope
- current on-hand and usable inventory
- projected days of cover
- expected demand window
- next confirmed inbound or transfer timing
- overstock risk category
- likely cause
- affected promotion, season, or campaign reference if relevant
- aging or slow-movement indicator where applicable
- current mitigation options
- priority classification
- response owner
- escalation status
- next review time
- final resolution

## 9. Standard Response Workflow

The normal overstock risk response workflow should follow this sequence:

1. detect the overstock risk signal
2. validate inventory, demand, and inbound facts
3. classify the risk type and urgency
4. assess business impact and affected scope
5. identify feasible mitigation options
6. prioritize the most material exposure
7. assign response owner
8. communicate to impacted teams
9. escalate if local action is insufficient
10. document final action, decision, and closure

The response should focus on reducing excess exposure while protecting overall business control and service balance.

## 10. Severity Levels

### Level 1 — Local manageable risk
Risk exists but can be handled through normal local adjustment or monitoring.

Examples:
- isolated excess at one store with moderate value
- one low-priority SKU with elevated cover but no immediate operational concern
- small inbound adjustment opportunity with limited wider impact

### Level 2 — Managed operational risk
Risk affects important inventory decisions and requires broader visibility.

Examples:
- moderate excess on important SKU
- confirmed inbound likely to worsen exposure
- repeated overstock risk on selected stores
- selected campaign residual stock issue

### Level 3 — Cross-functional inventory excess risk
Risk materially affects multiple locations, warehouse flow, inventory value exposure, or campaign recovery and requires coordinated action.

Examples:
- regional excess on high-volume SKU family
- warehouse congestion linked to slow-moving inventory
- forecast overestimation affecting multiple locations
- markdown, transfer, and replenishment adjustment needed together

### Level 4 — Executive or governance escalation
Risk creates major financial, operational, or policy exposure and requires exceptional action or approval.

Examples:
- strategic SKU family with major excess across regions
- severe warehouse capacity pressure due to excess stock
- large-value seasonal overhang
- controlled markdown, return, or policy exception decision required

---

## 11. Business Impact Assessment

Overstock risk cases should be assessed across the following areas.

### 11.1 Working capital impact
Will excess stock tie up material cash or reduce inventory productivity?

### 11.2 Warehouse and flow impact
Will excess inventory create congestion, storage inefficiency, or slower outbound flow?

### 11.3 Commercial impact
Will markdown, promotion adjustment, or sales support be needed to recover stock?

### 11.4 Inventory quality impact
Will aging, damage, expiry, or obsolescence risk increase if excess remains unaddressed?

### 11.5 Governance impact
Does the case require exception approval, executive review, or formal decision logging?

---

## 12. Immediate Mitigation Options

Depending on the situation, teams may consider:

- reducing or pausing short-term replenishment
- delaying or reshaping future inbound where feasible
- reviewing inter-location transfer options
- reprioritizing stock flow toward healthier demand locations
- increasing markdown or promotional support review where approved
- protecting warehouse capacity by controlling further inflow
- reviewing alternate channel or outlet options where approved
- increasing monitoring frequency until stock position stabilizes
- escalating for controlled policy exception or executive approval
- flagging the item or scope for urgent forecast and planning review

Any material deviation from normal policy must follow approved governance and approval paths.

---

## 13. Prioritization Rules During Overstock Risk

When overstock mitigation capacity is limited, prioritization should generally favor:

1. high-value or high-volume excess inventory
2. stock with aging, expiry, or obsolescence risk
3. inventory creating warehouse or flow pressure
4. campaign or seasonal residual stock with narrowing recovery window
5. lower-risk excess after higher-exposure inventory is addressed

Priority decisions should be documented when they materially change standard replenishment, transfer, or commercial behavior.

---

## 14. Communication Standards

When communicating an overstock risk, the message should include:

- what item or item group is at risk
- affected stores, warehouse, or region
- current excess position or projected overstock timing
- likely cause
- expected business impact
- actions already taken
- action or decision now needed
- owner
- next update time

Avoid vague messages such as:

- “inventory is too high”
- “there may be excess stock”
- “we have too much product”
- “please review inventory urgently”

Good communication must be clear, specific, and action-oriented.

---

## 15. Escalation Guidance

Escalation should be considered when:

- local reduction action is not enough
- multiple stores or a region are affected
- confirmed inbound will materially worsen exposure
- warehouse capacity or aging risk is becoming serious
- markdown, transfer, or allocation changes require management approval
- finance or commercial alignment is needed
- repeated excess pattern suggests structural weakness
- executive approval is needed for exception action

Escalation messages should state:

- issue summary
- affected business scope
- current timing and severity
- actions already taken
- decision or support required
- owner and timing

## 16. Investigation Guidance by Scenario

### 16.1 Forecast-linked overstock risk
Check:
- forecast versus actual demand
- recent overforecast pattern
- remaining inbound already committed
- whether excess is local or broad
- need for forecast correction or policy review

### 16.2 Promotion residual overstock
Check:
- campaign performance versus plan
- remaining event window
- store execution quality
- pricing and display support
- recovery options after campaign end

### 16.3 Slow-moving stock
Check:
- sell-through trend
- store or regional concentration
- life-cycle stage
- assortment change effect
- markdown or channel reallocation options

### 16.4 Inbound-driven overstock
Check:
- confirmed supplier timing
- order flexibility
- whether inbound can be delayed, reduced, or resequenced
- warehouse capacity effect
- finance and procurement alignment need

### 16.5 Warehouse overstock pressure
Check:
- storage utilization
- blocked or slow-moving areas
- impact on receiving and dispatch flow
- overflow usage
- urgency of stock recovery actions

### 16.6 Transfer imbalance
Check:
- source versus destination stock positions
- whether excess can be moved to healthier demand locations
- transport feasibility
- whether transfer would only shift the excess rather than solve it

## 18. Data Quality and Traceability Expectations

Overstock risk records should meet these standards:

- valid SKU and location scope
- usable timestamp
- current stock or cover reference
- known or estimated future inbound timing
- clear risk classification
- owner identity
- next review time for active case
- mitigation or escalation note
- final resolution captured for material cases

Poor-quality overstock risk records reduce response effectiveness and weaken trust in decision support.

## 19. Example Response Messages

### Example A — Store-level excess
**Subject:** Immediate review required for elevated cover at priority stores

**Message:**  
Stores 118 and 122 are showing projected cover materially above target for SKU 44219 based on current stock and recent demand trend. The issue appears linked to lower-than-expected sell-through following the last replenishment cycle. Planning should review short-term replenishment reduction immediately, and regional operations should confirm whether local execution factors are slowing movement.

### Example B — Regional overstock risk
**Subject:** Regional overstock risk emerging for beverage SKU family

**Message:**  
West region now shows elevated cover across six beverage SKUs in multiple stores, with future inbound likely to worsen the current position if no action is taken. The issue appears broader than one location and may require coordinated adjustment to replenishment, transfer, and commercial support decisions. Planning, procurement, and regional operations coordination is required today.

### Example C — Promotion residual stock
**Subject:** Campaign residual inventory requires recovery review

**Message:**  
A featured snack SKU in the approved weekend campaign remains materially above expected residual stock level in the North region after demand performed below plan. Current stock position is likely to create excess cover and working capital pressure if standard replenishment continues unchanged. Commercial and planning teams should review markdown, transfer, or temporary replenishment reduction options before 2:00 PM today.

### Example D — Executive escalation
**Subject:** Executive approval required for major overstock risk response

**Message:**  
A major overstock risk now affects a strategic SKU family across two regions, with confirmed future inbound likely to increase financial and warehouse exposure further. Standard replenishment behavior is not sufficient to contain the risk. Planning recommends temporary controlled action across replenishment, transfer, and commercial recovery pathways. Executive direction is required before 4:00 PM today.

## 20. Non-Compliant Response Examples

The following are poor practices:

- continuing normal replenishment despite clear projected excess
- assuming excess inventory will resolve without evidence
- allowing confirmed inbound to worsen avoidable overstock with no review
- changing markdown or transfer behavior without documented reasoning
- failing to inform impacted teams about material excess exposure
- using vague “high stock” messages without timing or scope
- not reviewing aging or warehouse pressure when excess is already visible
