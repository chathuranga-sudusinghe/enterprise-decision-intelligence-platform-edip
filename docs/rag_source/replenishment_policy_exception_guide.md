## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the standard approach for identifying, reviewing, approving, communicating, and resolving replenishment policy exceptions at NorthStar Retail & Distribution (NRD).

Its purpose is to ensure that when the business needs to act outside normal replenishment policy rules, that action is handled in a controlled, traceable, and business-justified way rather than through informal, inconsistent, or undocumented decision-making.

This guide supports the Enterprise Decision Intelligence Platform (EDIP) by providing governed knowledge for:

- replenishment policy exception handling
- override and exception review
- temporary deviation from standard planning rules
- escalation and approval support
- cross-functional operational coordination
- audit-aware decision traceability

---

## 2. Scope

This guide applies to replenishment policy exceptions involving:

- deviation from normal reorder logic
- deviation from minimum or maximum stock rules
- temporary change to order frequency or cadence
- exception to allocation priorities
- temporary relaxation of service rules
- temporary bypass of standard replenishment constraints
- exception to lead time assumption in operational decisioning
- manual intervention outside normal planning thresholds
- temporary policy deviation during major disruption, campaign, or trading event
- repeated exception behavior requiring governance review

This guide applies to teams such as:

- supply chain planning
- demand planning
- regional operations
- warehouse operations
- procurement
- store operations
- inventory control
- commercial teams where campaigns or launches are affected
- executive stakeholders for major policy exception cases

---

## 3. Definition of Replenishment Policy Exception

A replenishment policy exception exists when the business intentionally decides to act outside the standard approved replenishment rule set in order to manage a specific operational, commercial, or risk-based situation.

Examples include:

- ordering above normal maximum to protect a major trading event
- reducing normal order flow because warehouse capacity cannot support standard policy execution
- changing allocation priority during temporary shortage
- overriding standard reorder timing due to supplier or transport disruption
- applying non-standard store prioritization during service risk
- temporarily adjusting policy logic for campaign support
- bypassing standard replenishment thresholds to stabilize a regional issue

A replenishment policy exception is not just a planner preference.  
It is a governed deviation from standard operating logic and must be justified.

---

## 4. Core Principles

All replenishment policy exceptions must follow these principles.

### 4.1 Business justification first
An exception must be supported by a clear business reason, not personal preference or convenience.

### 4.2 Temporary and controlled use
Exceptions should be used only as needed and should not silently become normal practice.

### 4.3 Least necessary deviation
The business should use the smallest exception required to manage the situation effectively.

### 4.4 Cross-functional awareness when impact is broad
If the exception affects stores, warehouses, procurement, campaigns, or regions materially, the relevant teams must be informed.

### 4.5 Approval discipline
Material exceptions must follow the appropriate approval path.

### 4.6 Full traceability
Exception reason, owner, timing, impact, and approval status must be documented appropriately.

## 5. Typical Reasons for Policy Exceptions

Replenishment policy exceptions may arise from:

- supplier delay or supply disruption
- warehouse capacity constraint
- transfer execution failure
- short-term demand spike
- forecast error creating immediate service risk
- promotion or campaign support need
- strategic or high-visibility product protection
- regional disruption or local operational issue
- inventory accuracy concern requiring temporary caution
- extreme weather or event-related business condition
- executive direction during unusual trading period
- temporary constraint in transport or receiving capability

The actual reason should be clearly recorded before the exception is approved.

---

## 6. Common Exception Categories

### 6.1 Quantity exception
Order quantity is intentionally increased or decreased outside the normal recommended range.

### 6.2 Timing exception
Replenishment timing is changed outside the normal cycle or cadence.

### 6.3 Priority exception
Standard store, region, or channel priority is changed temporarily.

### 6.4 Constraint override exception
Standard replenishment constraints are relaxed or bypassed under controlled approval.

### 6.5 Campaign support exception
Normal policy is adjusted temporarily to support promotion, launch, or seasonal event demand.

### 6.6 Risk mitigation exception
Policy is adjusted to reduce imminent stockout, service failure, or operational disruption.

### 6.7 Structural review exception
Repeated need for the same exception indicates the standard policy may require redesign.

---

## 7. Trigger Conditions

A replenishment issue should be treated as a formal policy exception case when one or more of the following apply:

- standard policy will not protect required service outcome
- standard logic creates unacceptable stockout or overstock risk in current conditions
- major campaign or strategic event cannot be supported under normal rules
- warehouse, supplier, transport, or store constraint blocks standard execution
- multiple stores, regions, or high-priority SKUs are affected
- the required decision falls outside normal planner discretion
- cross-functional business impact is material
- repeated similar exceptions are occurring
- executive or governance approval may be required
- audit traceability is needed due to business sensitivity

## 8. Required Assessment Fields

Every material replenishment policy exception review should capture, where available:

- exception ID or reference
- date and time of request
- SKU / item ID and description
- affected store, warehouse, region, or channel scope
- current recommended action under standard policy
- requested exception action
- exception category
- business reason
- expected start date and end date
- estimated business impact
- risk if exception is not approved
- approval owner
- escalation status
- next review time
- final decision and outcome

---

## 9. Standard Exception Workflow

The normal replenishment policy exception workflow should follow this sequence:

1. identify the need for policy deviation
2. validate the operational and business facts
3. classify the exception type and urgency
4. assess scope, risk, and expected impact
5. determine whether local discretion is sufficient or approval is required
6. assign exception owner
7. communicate to impacted teams where needed
8. approve, reject, or modify the exception request
9. monitor the exception outcome while active
10. document closure and lessons if needed

The workflow should protect both business agility and governance integrity.

## 10. Severity Levels

### Level 1 — Local controlled exception
Exception is limited in scope and can be handled within defined local authority.

Examples:
- small quantity adjustment for one low-risk SKU
- isolated timing deviation with minimal wider impact
- short-lived store-level exception with clear reason

### Level 2 — Managed operational exception
Exception affects important items or multiple locations and requires broader review or visibility.

Examples:
- non-standard allocation for selected stores
- above-threshold quantity increase on important SKU
- temporary cycle change affecting several locations
- moderate campaign support exception

### Level 3 — Cross-functional policy exception
Exception materially affects multiple teams, major service outcomes, or regional flow and requires coordinated approval.

Examples:
- regional priority change during stock shortage
- warehouse-driven constraint override affecting replenishment pattern
- temporary policy shift for major campaign or event
- repeated large-scale deviation from standard policy

### Level 4 — Executive or governance exception
Exception creates major business, financial, or governance exposure and requires senior approval.

Examples:
- strategic SKU allocation override across regions
- major temporary suspension of normal policy logic
- exceptional replenishment action ahead of high-stakes trading period
- repeated exception dependence indicating policy failure

---

## 11. Business Impact Assessment

Replenishment policy exception cases should be assessed across the following areas.

### 11.1 Service impact
Will the exception improve or protect availability, or does rejection create service risk?

### 11.2 Inventory impact
Will the exception increase overstock, imbalance, or stock positioning risk?

### 11.3 Operational impact
Will stores, warehouses, procurement, or transport need to work differently?

### 11.4 Commercial impact
Will the decision affect campaign readiness, strategic items, or revenue-critical demand?

### 11.5 Governance impact
Does the case require controlled approval, executive review, or formal decision logging?

---

## 12. Exception Evaluation Questions

Before approving a replenishment policy exception, teams should ask:

- What standard policy outcome would happen without the exception?
- Why is that standard outcome not acceptable in this case?
- Is the exception temporary or likely to repeat?
- What is the smallest safe deviation needed?
- Which locations, items, or channels are affected?
- What business benefit is expected?
- What risk does the exception create?
- Does the exception require cross-functional awareness?
- Who must approve it?
- How will the exception be monitored and closed?

These questions help prevent unnecessary or weakly justified deviations.

---

## 13. Typical Response Actions

Depending on the case, teams may consider:

- temporary quantity uplift or reduction
- temporary cadence adjustment
- controlled store or regional reprioritization
- temporary allocation change
- temporary relaxation of reorder rule
- temporary protection for promotion-support stock
- temporary block on lower-priority replenishment
- increased monitoring until the exception ends
- escalation for higher approval where required
- formal review of policy design if repeated exception need is visible

Any material deviation from standard policy must follow approved governance and approval paths.

---

## 14. Prioritization Rules During Exception Handling

When exception requests compete or business flexibility is limited, prioritization should generally favor:

1. service-critical and high-velocity SKUs
2. stores or regions facing imminent service failure
3. active promotion, launch, or strategic demand windows
4. cases with the clearest business justification and controlled duration
5. normal lower-priority exceptions after higher-risk exposure is addressed

Priority decisions should be documented when they materially change standard replenishment behavior.

---

## 15. Communication Standards

When communicating a replenishment policy exception, the message should include:

- what standard policy result is being changed
- requested exception action
- affected item and scope
- business reason
- expected duration
- expected business impact
- actions already reviewed
- decision or approval needed
- owner
- next update time if active

Avoid vague messages such as:

- “please override policy”
- “system recommendation is not good”
- “we need to change ordering”
- “urgent exception needed”

Good communication must be clear, specific, and action-oriented.

---

## 16. Escalation Guidance

Escalation should be considered when:

- the exception exceeds local authority
- multiple stores or regions are affected
- the decision changes strategic allocation or campaign support materially
- warehouse, procurement, and planning all need coordinated alignment
- financial or governance exposure is meaningful
- repeated exception use indicates structural policy weakness
- executive approval is needed for the requested deviation

Escalation messages should state:

- issue summary
- affected business scope
- requested exception
- why standard policy is not sufficient
- actions already taken
- decision or support required
- owner and timing

---

## 17. Investigation Guidance by Scenario

### 17.1 Quantity exception
Check:
- current demand and cover position
- whether the uplift or reduction is temporary
- warehouse and supplier support feasibility
- overstock or stockout risk created by the change
- whether a smaller deviation would be sufficient

### 17.2 Timing exception
Check:
- urgency of the required timing change
- transport and receiving feasibility
- whether service risk justifies the deviation
- whether the timing change affects other planned flows

### 17.3 Priority exception
Check:
- why standard priority ranking is insufficient
- which stores or regions are gaining or losing support
- whether the change is temporary and fair under policy intent
- whether executive visibility is needed

### 17.4 Campaign support exception
Check:
- campaign timing and scale
- inventory position
- standard policy gap
- risk of overreaction after campaign demand ends
- commercial coordination need

### 17.5 Structural repeat exception
Check:
- history of similar exception requests
- same item, region, or process pattern
- whether policy design, data input, or operational model is misaligned
- whether governance review is required

## 18. Link to EDIP Decisioning

Replenishment policy exceptions matter to EDIP because they can directly affect:

- replenishment recommendations
- transfer suggestions
- stockout and overstock risk interpretation
- planner override behavior
- allocation priorities
- promotion support readiness
- executive exception decisioning

For this reason, material policy exception events should be visible to relevant planning, operations, procurement, and governance functions when they alter downstream decisions.

## 19. Data Quality and Traceability Expectations

Replenishment policy exception records should meet these standards:

- valid exception reference
- valid item and location scope
- usable timestamp
- clear standard-versus-exception description
- clear reason and expected duration
- owner identity
- approval status
- next review time for active case
- mitigation or monitoring note
- final closure and outcome captured for material cases

Poor-quality exception records reduce governance quality and weaken trust in decision support.

## 20. Example Exception Messages

### Example A — Quantity exception
**Subject:** Approval required for temporary replenishment quantity exception

**Message:**  
Standard replenishment logic for SKU 44219 in the West region currently recommends order quantities below the level needed to protect service through the upcoming demand window. Planning requests a temporary quantity exception for the next two cycles to reduce projected stockout risk across five high-velocity stores. Warehouse support feasibility has been reviewed, and approval is required by 1:00 PM today.

### Example B — Timing exception
**Subject:** Temporary replenishment timing exception required for service recovery

**Message:**  
A timing exception is requested for selected North region stores because the normal replenishment cycle will not recover service in time after the recent supplier-linked disruption. The proposed deviation would advance replenishment timing for a short controlled period until inbound flow stabilizes. Planning and warehouse operations have reviewed feasibility. Regional approval is requested by end of day.

### Example C — Priority exception
**Subject:** Regional priority exception review needed for constrained supply allocation

**Message:**  
Current constrained supply conditions require a temporary exception to standard store allocation priorities for a high-velocity beverage SKU family. Without this change, two priority store clusters face earlier stockout exposure during the weekend trading window. Planning requests approval for controlled reprioritization through Sunday close of business. Cross-functional visibility is required due to customer service impact.

### Example D — Executive escalation
**Subject:** Executive approval required for major replenishment policy exception

**Message:**  
A major replenishment policy exception is requested for a strategic SKU family across two regions ahead of a key trading period. Standard policy behavior is not sufficient to protect availability under current supplier and warehouse constraints. Planning recommends a temporary controlled deviation from normal allocation and timing rules. Executive direction is required before 4:00 PM today.

---

## 21. Non-Compliant Handling Examples

The following are poor practices:

- applying a policy deviation without a recorded reason
- using exception logic as informal daily practice
- failing to state start and end timing for temporary exception
- overriding standard behavior with no approval where approval is required
- not informing impacted teams about material policy change
- repeating the same exception with no structural review
- documenting only “business need” without explaining actual impact

## 25. Summary

NRD requires replenishment policy exceptions to be handled with clear business justification, disciplined approval, controlled duration, cross-functional awareness where needed, and proper traceability.
